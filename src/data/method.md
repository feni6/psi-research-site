# Curation Methodology

## What this library is

A curated collection of 354 scientific publications on psi phenomena — predominantly peer-reviewed journal articles, with a small number of published books, book chapters, and preprints from recognized repositories. The collection covers telepathy, precognition, psychokinesis, remote viewing, healing intention, mediumship, near-death experiences, critical and skeptical literature, and methodological discussions. The papers span 1886 to 2025, drawn from 118 journals, and include both supportive and critical work: 49 papers (13.8%) occupy the Skeptical and Critical folder. The collection was designed from the outset to be navigable by both human readers and AI agents, with a machine-readable master catalog, structured summaries alongside every PDF, debate maps presenting both sides of contested questions, and curated reading paths. It is not an advocacy resource for or against psi claims; it is a structured knowledge base intended for serious engagement with a contested scientific domain.

## Who built it and how

The prompt that generated the creation of the plan to build this library was: "I'm building a library of publications about "psi" phenomena, as discussed in books by Dean Radin and in the recent Telepathy Tapes podcast.   I want you to come up with a plan for building this library on my computer, on my desktop, in a way that captures the most relevant, rigorous, and recent scientific work and keeps things very well organized. The plan should not include subscribing to any journals or paying for access to data.  The plan should be as specific as possible, including specific websites where data/papers can be obtained. The plan should also include a way to organize the data obtained on the desktop in a way that it will be most easily understood by AI agents and large language models."  The result was Psi_Research_Library_Plan.md.  The human prompter made small modifications to the plan afterwards but no substantive changes.  

The human curator then directed AI agents (Anthropic's Claude Code running the Opus 4.6 llm on high-reasoning mode) through a set of structured planning documents generated to preceduralize Psi_Research_Library_Plan.md. Thus Claude designed the library's architecture — 13 topic folders, a JSON catalog schema, knowledge-structure files for controversies, reading paths, replication lineages, and a historical timeline — and proposed the scope, editorial policy, seed sources, and quality standards. The AI agents executed the plan: downloading papers through scripted pipelines, reading full PDFs using PyMuPDF, extracting and verifying metadata, writing structured `.summary.json` sidecars, populating catalog entries with effect sizes and statistics from paper text, mining citations from reference sections, maintaining cross-reference links, updating controversy maps and reading paths, and running validation checks. Three governing documents defined the workflow: `Psi_Research_Library_Plan.md` (the overall architecture and vision), `_PAPER_PROCESSING_GUIDE.md` (a 10-step per-paper processing pipeline), and `_Staging_Plan.md` (the sourcing and download strategy).

The library was built iteratively over 388 sessions across approximately one week (late February to early March 2026). Each session typically processed one paper through the full pipeline. Quality standards evolved: early papers were batch-imported as minimal stubs with only a filename and folder assignment; over time, processing requirements tightened until every entry demanded full-text reading, a 400–800 character abstract, a minimum of 3 key findings with specific statistics, a minimum of 3 independently assessed limitations, all methodology fields populated from the paper text, and all cross-reference IDs verified against the catalog. This evolution is documented — `_SESSION_STATE.md` records every session's actions, and earlier stub entries were revisited and upgraded as standards rose. As of the current catalog, all 354 entries have abstracts, substantive notes, tags, and related-paper links. A selection rule ensures that unprocessed papers are always handled before already-processed ones, preventing repeated polishing of the same entries while others remain unread.

## Paper selection

### Seed sources

The initial paper list was defined in `Psi_Research_Library_Plan.md` and seeded from several sources: Dean Radin's curated reference list at deanradin.com (the plan's designated "single best starting point"), the Journal of Scientific Exploration's full open-access archive, PubMed Central and Frontiers journals for open-access biomedical and psychology literature, Google Scholar and Semantic Scholar for targeted title searches, and author websites and institutional repositories including ResearchGate, Academia.edu, and university faculty pages. The seed sources drew heavily from reference lists curated by researchers sympathetic to psi research. This is an acknowledged bias — the library's nucleus reflects the field as seen by its proponents — and was partly counterbalanced by deliberate inclusion of skeptical literature (see below) and by expansion through citation mining.

### Expansion via citation mining

After the initial seeding, the library grew through structured citation mining. Each time a paper was fully processed, its reference section was read and checked against both the catalog and the wishlist. Papers meeting any of four criteria were added to the wishlist: the paper is foundational to the paradigm and cited by multiple library papers; the paper would fill a genuine gap such as the only skeptical voice on a one-sided controversy; the paper is the explicit target of a critique already in the library; or the paper is the direct historical precursor to an experimental program represented in the library. Additions were capped at 3–5 papers per processed paper to prevent unbounded growth. Because citation mining proceeds from papers already in the collection, and the collection's nucleus is pro-psi, the references discovered tend to reflect psi-sympathetic citation networks; the second selection criterion (filling genuine gaps) partially counteracts this by actively seeking missing skeptical voices. Each wishlist entry includes a `why_needed` field documenting the justification. The wishlist currently contains 514 items, each recording why the paper was sought and what acquisition sources were tried.

### Skeptical and critical literature

The library was deliberately designed to include critical voices. Folder `10_Skeptical_and_Critical` collects critiques, failed replications, and debunking literature — currently 49 papers, the third-largest folder. The controversy maps in `_CONTROVERSIES/` present papers on both sides of each debate with equal structural weight. One reading path (`path_2_weak_spots`) guides sympathetic readers through the strongest critiques and failure points first. Step 7 of the original build workflow specifically targets skeptical literature from researchers including Hyman, Wagenmakers, Rouder, Alcock, and Vyse. Despite these efforts, the skeptical ratio stands at 13.8%, below the 20% target documented in the session state's progress table. The gap reflects both the seed-source tilt and a structural access problem: skeptical papers are more likely to appear in mainstream psychology journals with restrictive paywalls.

## Sourcing and acquisition

### Prioritization

Not all wishlist papers were pursued simultaneously. The AI agents triaged the wishlist into priority batches ordered by expected yield, working the highest-probability targets first. Batch 1 targeted papers with known DOIs (33 items, ~60% expected hit rate), since DOI resolution through open-access APIs is the single most reliable retrieval method. Batch 2 targeted papers from the Journal of Scientific Exploration (16 items, ~70% expected hit rate), whose full archive is open access. Batch 3 targeted papers from always-open-access publishers — Frontiers, PLoS ONE, F1000Research (~100% expected hit rate). Batch 4 targeted books likely held by the Internet Archive (12 items, ~50% expected hit rate). Batch 5 swept author websites and ResearchGate profiles for remaining high-priority items. Batch 6 applied creative workarounds — preprints, the Wayback Machine, conference proceedings, chapter extraction from full books — to papers that had resisted all standard approaches. Batch 7 flagged papers with no viable open-access path for manual acquisition (author email, library access, or purchase). Within each batch, the wishlist's own priority field (high, medium, or low — assigned based on the paper's importance to the library's coverage goals) determined processing order.

### Sourcing tiers

For each paper, the agents worked through a six-tier sourcing strategy defined in `_Staging_Plan.md`: (1) DOI resolution via the Unpaywall, CrossRef, and Semantic Scholar APIs and PubMed Central; (2) journal-specific archives including the JSE's full open-access collection, Frontiers, PLoS ONE, and F1000Research; (3) author and lab websites (deanradin.com, sheldrake.org, noosphere.princeton.edu, jeksite.org, richardwiseman.com, ResearchGate, Academia.edu); (4) Internet Archive and Open Library for books and pre-2000 works; (5) specialized archives such as the Parapsychological Association, CIA STAR GATE reading room, and the Princeton PEAR Lab; (6) creative workarounds including preprints on PsyArXiv/OSF/arXiv, the Wayback Machine, thesis repositories, and Google Scholar's "All versions." All papers were obtained through legal open-access channels.


## Per-paper processing

Each paper passes through a 10-step pipeline defined in `_PAPER_PROCESSING_GUIDE.md`. Selection prioritizes unprocessed papers — any catalog entry lacking a `.summary.json` sidecar is chosen before entries already through the pipeline. The selected paper is read in full using PyMuPDF (not just the abstract — methods, results, discussion, and references). The agent analyzes paper type, stance, author list, findings, methodology, and citation network, then writes a structured `.summary.json` sidecar with eight required fields and rewrites the catalog entry with verified metadata. The reference section is mined for important missing papers (capped at 3–5 per paper), back-links are added to related entries, and knowledge-structure files are updated when warranted. The automated validator checks structural consistency, and the session state is updated with a log of actions taken.

The processing guide enforces specific quality thresholds. Abstracts must be 400–800 characters covering the question, method, key numerical results, and conclusion. Key findings require a minimum of 3 entries with actual numbers from the results section. Limitations require a minimum of 3 entries specific to the paper, including at least one the authors did not acknowledge. All methodology fields — sample size, effect size, confidence interval, Bayes factor, blinding, pre-registration status — must be populated from the paper text, with `null` only where the paper genuinely does not report a value. Cross-reference IDs are verified against the catalog before inclusion. A list of 10 common mistakes, developed from errors caught during earlier sessions, is checked against each entry before it is saved.

## What was excluded

### By scope

The library includes peer-reviewed journal articles, published book chapters, and preprints from recognized repositories (arXiv, PsyArXiv, OSF, university institutional repositories). It excludes popular press, blog posts, unreviewed manuscripts, podcasts, and social media content. Conference proceedings from the Parapsychological Association are included when available as formal papers. Dissertations are included only when they are the sole source for a significant study.

### By access

Only freely and legally available papers were included. The library was built entirely from open-access journals, author-posted manuscripts, institutional repositories, and public archives. Papers behind paywalls that could not be obtained through any legal channel were not included. Each unfound paper remains on the wishlist (`_WISHLIST.json`) with a record of every source tried, the result of each attempt, and a recommended next step. The wishlist currently contains 514 items. It is itself a transparency mechanism: it shows every gap the curator is aware of and documents the effort made to fill each one.

### Not excluded: papers by conclusion

Papers were not filtered by whether their findings support or challenge psi claims. The library includes positive results, null results, failed replications, methodological critiques, statistical reanalyses, and theoretical objections. Both sides receive the same processing treatment: identical metadata standards, identical summary depth, identical placement in the controversy maps.

## Known biases and limitations

The initial paper list drew heavily from Dean Radin's curated references and IONS publications, both sympathetic to psi research. The library's nucleus therefore reflects the field as seen by its proponents — the earliest papers acquired were disproportionately those that psi advocates consider important. Mitigation included a dedicated build-workflow step targeting skeptical literature, active wishlist entries seeking critical papers, and a folder reserved for skeptical and critical work. The skeptical ratio currently stands at 13.8%, below the 20% target. The seed-source bias has been partially but not fully corrected. An additional upstream factor is publication bias within psi research itself: positive results are more likely to be published than null results. Any collection of published literature inherits this tilt regardless of curatorial choices; the inclusion of failed replications partially counteracts it but cannot correct for what was never published.

Because only freely available papers were included, the collection skews toward open-access journals (Frontiers, JSE, PLoS ONE, F1000Research) and author-posted manuscripts. Paywalled outlets — including mainstream psychology journals like Psychological Bulletin, Psychological Review, and The Lancet — are underrepresented. This disproportionately affects skeptical literature, which is more likely to appear in journals with restrictive access policies, and landmark papers in high-prestige venues. Some important paywalled works by Hyman, Alcock, Wagenmakers, and others were obtainable through author websites; others remain on the wishlist. This bias is structural and cannot be fully corrected without institutional library access or purchase.

Catalog entries, structured summaries, and methodology fields were generated by AI agents reading PDFs, not by domain experts. While the processing guide enforces quality standards — specific numbers required, IDs verified, full-text reading mandatory, 10 documented common mistakes to avoid — AI-generated metadata may contain errors: misidentified effect sizes, misclassified stances, missed nuances in complex statistical arguments, or hallucinated cross-reference connections. Automated validation catches structural errors, and the richness-scoring system surfaces entries needing reprocessing. The `.summary.json` sidecars and catalog entries should be treated as aids to navigation, not as authoritative interpretations of the papers they describe.

The collection focuses on post-2000 empirical research, with selected historical landmarks reaching back to 1886. Pre-1970 parapsychology — the Rhine era, early SPR work — is underrepresented relative to its historical importance, and the literature is predominantly Anglophone and Western. The temporal skew is partly deliberate (the plan prioritizes modern empirical work), but it means the library is a weaker resource for historical claims about the field than for evaluating the current state of the evidence.

The library originated from a prompt that explicitly named Dean Radin and the Telepathy Tapes podcast as starting points — both sympathetic to psi claims. A curator starting from a skeptical position would likely have written a different prompt and made different architectural choices — foregrounding methodological critiques more prominently, structuring reading paths around failure-to-replicate narratives, or allocating less space to domains like healing intention and mediumship. Mitigation: the library's structure explicitly accommodates skeptical engagement through controversy maps, a dedicated reading path for counter-evidence, and folder 10; the processing guide applies identical quality standards regardless of a paper's conclusions; and this methodology statement is itself an attempt at transparency. No curation is perspective-free. This library is honest about its starting point and invites scrutiny of its choices.

## Transparency mechanisms

The library was designed to be auditable. Every significant decision, attempt, and failure is recorded in a file that any reviewer can inspect. `_CATALOG.json` contains the full metadata for all 354 papers, allowing any individual entry to be checked against its PDF. `_WISHLIST.json` documents 514 papers sought but not yet acquired, with sources tried and reasons for each gap. `_STAGING/_MANIFEST.json` records 238 sourcing attempts including HTTP status codes, validation results, and content verification outcomes. `_SESSION_STATE.md` logs all 388 processing sessions with specific actions taken in each. The `_CONTROVERSIES/` directory maps 14 debates with papers cited on both sides, allowing reviewers to check whether the framing is balanced. Each paper's `.summary.json` sidecar provides a structured summary that can be compared against the actual PDF. `_validate_catalog.py` is a runnable script that verifies catalog-to-disk consistency, required fields, naming conventions, and duplicate IDs. `_PAPER_PROCESSING_GUIDE.md` documents the quality standards applied to every entry. `_Staging_Plan.md` records the sourcing strategy, including a frank account of Round 1 failures and how they were addressed.

## How to verify or challenge this library

Pick any catalog entry, read the PDF, and compare the metadata and `.summary.json` against what the paper actually says — the most direct test of quality. Check the controversy maps for balance: are both sides represented, and is the framing fair? Review the wishlist for important omissions: are there papers that should have been sought but were not? Run `python3 _validate_catalog.py` to verify structural consistency. Check the skeptical ratio — is the critical literature proportionate to its role in the scientific debate? If you find errors, they are genuine errors, not intentional distortions; the processing guide's "Common Mistakes to Avoid" section documents the kinds of mistakes that have been caught and corrected throughout the library's construction. This library was designed to withstand examination, not to discourage it.
