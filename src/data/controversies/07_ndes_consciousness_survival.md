# 7. Are NDEs evidence for consciousness survival?

Near-death experiences (NDEs) -- vivid, structured experiences reported by individuals who come close to or reach clinical death -- are cited by some researchers as evidence that consciousness can function independently of the brain. Skeptics argue NDEs are fully explicable by neurological, pharmacological, and psychological mechanisms during brain crisis.

**Pro:**
- Moody (1977) -- Foundational sequel to Life After Life (1975) documenting hundreds of additional NDE interviews; identifies new elements (Vision of Knowledge, Cities of Light, Realm of Bewildered Spirits, Supernatural Rescues); demonstrates multiple independent investigators found identical patterns; includes methodological appendix outlining controlled study designs for future NDE research [catalog ID: moody_1977_reflections]
- Greyson (1983) -- Construction and validation of the 16-item NDE Scale: first quantitative instrument for measuring NDEs with demonstrated reliability (alpha=.88, test-retest r=.92) and validity (r=.90 with Ring's WCEI); established 4 components (Cognitive, Affective, Paranormal, Transcendental); cut-off score of 7 identified 83.8% of self-reported NDEs; scale differentiates NDEs from organic brain syndromes and stress responses, enabling subsequent empirical research [catalog ID: greyson_1983_nde_scale]
- Ring & Cooper (1997) -- First systematic study of NDEs/OBEs in 31 blind respondents (14 blind from birth): 80% reported visual perceptions during experiences, including 64% of those congenitally blind; one corroborated case (hospital corridor confirmed by independent witness) and one suggestive but inconclusive case (tie description); authors reject dream, blindsight, and retrospective reconstruction hypotheses, proposing 'transcendental awareness' [catalog ID: ring_1997_near_death_blind]
- van Lommel et al. (2001) -- Prospective study of NDEs in cardiac arrest survivors (N=344); 18% report NDEs, some with verified out-of-body perceptions, published in The Lancet [catalog ID: lommel_2001_neardeath]
- van Lommel (2006) -- Extended analysis of the implications of NDEs for the mind-brain relationship [catalog ID: lommel_2006_neardeath]
- van Lommel (2013) -- Comprehensive narrative review proposing "non-local consciousness": synthesizes the Dutch prospective study (18% NDE incidence, N=344) with neurophysiological evidence that EEG flatlines within 15 seconds of cardiac arrest and CPR provides only 5-10% of normal cerebral blood flow; argues no physiological, psychological, or pharmacological variable explains NDE occurrence; 8-year longitudinal follow-up shows lasting transformation; proposes brain as transceiver/interface rather than producer of consciousness [catalog ID: van_lommel_2013_nonlocal_consciousness]
- Greyson (2003) -- Largest single-center prospective cardiac NDE survey: 1,595 patients over 30 months; 10% of cardiac arrest survivors reported NDEs vs 1% with other cardiac diagnoses (P<.001); NDErs younger and more likely to have lost consciousness, but no differences on objective physiological measures (cardiac dysfunction, proximity to death, coronary prognosis) [catalog ID: greyson_2003_incidence_nde_cardiac]
- Parnia et al. (2014) -- The AWARE study: largest prospective NDE study in cardiac arrest patients across 15 hospitals; some patients report verified perceptions during documented periods of cardiac arrest [catalog ID: parnia_2014_aware]
- Greyson (2010) -- "Peak in Darien" cases: reviews historical and contemporary NDEs where experiencers see deceased persons whose death was unknown to anyone present; 21% of 665 NDEs included deceased encounters vs. 4% with living persons [catalog ID: greyson_2010_peak_in_darien]
- Greyson (2007) -- First long-term quantitative study of NDE memory reliability: 72 experiencers re-tested on NDE Scale after mean 19.1 years showed no significant change on total score (T1=14.60, T2=14.24, t(71)=0.69, p=0.49), all 4 factors, or all 16 items; test-retest r=0.83 (p<0.001); score changes not correlated with time elapsed; positive affect showed nonsignificant DECLINE rather than embellishment. Published in *Resuscitation* [catalog ID: greyson_2007_consistency_nde]
- Greyson (2011) -- "Cosmological Implications of Near-Death Experiences" reviews NDE evidence during cardiac arrest (12-18% of survivors report NDEs despite flat-line EEG) and anesthesia (22% of UVA collection), argues enhanced mentation, veridical OBE (92% accuracy per Holden 2009), and Peak in Darien cases require a revised cosmology beyond materialist reductionism [catalog ID: greyson_2011_western_approaches_nde]
- Facco, Agrillo & Greyson (2015) -- Systematic epistemological critique of neurobiological NDE explanations (retinal ischemia, opioids, NMDA, temporal lobe epilepsy, REM intrusion); NDE incidence 10-18% in critical patients; NDE memories show theta-band EEG consistent with true episodic memory; proposes 'Non-Ordinary Mental Expressions' (NOMEs) framework to replace 'altered states of consciousness' [catalog ID: facco_2015_nde_epistemological]
- Long & Holden (2007) -- Point-by-point critique of Nelson et al.'s (2006) REM-intrusion NDE hypothesis: challenges the unvalidated NDE Index, unvalidated REM Intrusion Survey, unmatched control group (mean age 49.4 vs. 36.4), and lack of multiple-comparisons correction; NDERF data show 73.5% of NDErs report experience was "more real than real," inconsistent with dream-like REM phenomenology [catalog ID: long_holden_2007_rem_nde_response]
- Facco & Agrillo (2012) -- "Hypothesis and Theory" article by an ICU anesthesiologist who systematically examines eight neurobiological explanations for NDEs (retinal ischemia, CO₂, temporal lobe, opioids, hallucinogens, REM intrusion, G-forces, psychological expectation) and argues that none adequately accounts for NDE phenomenology; explicitly critiques Mobbs & Watt (2011) for offering analogies without identity; calls for epistemological neutrality rather than a priori physicalism; reviewed and approved by Mobbs himself alongside Greyson and van Lommel [catalog ID: facco_2012_neardeath]
- Thonnard et al. (2013) -- NDE memories scored significantly higher on the MCQ than all other memory types including real events (H(3,N=39)=20.57, p<0.001); more self-referential, emotional, and clarity characteristics than coma or childhood memories (all p<0.005); proposed as flashbulb memories of hallucinations [catalog ID: thonnard_2013_nde_memory_reality]
- Cassol et al. (2018) -- Qualitative thematic analysis of 34 cardiac arrest survivors' NDE narratives identified 11 themes (Cohen's kappa = 0.73); 7 match Greyson Scale features, 3 additional themes (Entrance, Return, Description of scenes) extend beyond existing questionnaires; supports NDEs as 'universal human experiences' with consistent phenomenological structure [catalog ID: cassol_2018_nde_memory]
- Cassol et al. (2019) -- First systematic empirical replication of Greyson & Bush's (1992) distressing NDE taxonomy: 14% of 123 NDErs reported distressing experiences (8 inverse, 8 hellish, 1 void; inter-rater kappa 0.855–1.0); distressing NDE memories equally vivid and phenomenologically detailed as classical ones on all MCQ dimensions (Bayesian evidence for null on 14/15 items); supports NDEs as robust experiential phenomena regardless of valence [catalog ID: cassol_2019_distressing_nde]
- Moore & Greyson (2017) -- Largest MCQ study of NDE memory (N=122): repeated measures ANOVA (F=113.67, η²=0.486) shows NDE memories scored significantly higher than both real-event and imagined-event memories on 4 of 5 MCQ factors (all pairwise p<.001); NDE depth correlates with memory richness (r=0.42, p<.001); extends Thonnard et al. (2013) with 3× larger sample and adds non-NDEr comparison group [catalog ID: moore_2017_nde_memory]
- Martial et al. (2017) -- Survey of 152 NDErs shows Greyson NDE scale total score positively correlated with MCQ total score (r=0.29, p<0.0005); intensity specifically associated with sensory details, personal importance, and reactivation frequency; no association with time since NDE (mean 23 years), supporting memory stability [catalog ID: martial_2017_intensity_memory]
- Palmieri et al. (2014) -- Integrated psychodynamic-EEG study (N=20) showing NDE memories are phenomenologically indistinguishable from real memories (p=0.185) but neurally distinct: NDE recall uniquely correlated with delta (r=0.778) and theta bands, while real memories correlated with high alpha and gamma; hypnosis enhanced all memory types but NDE memories remained distinct [catalog ID: palmieri_2014_reality_nde]
- Woollacott & Shumway-Cook (2020) -- Narrative review comparing NDEs, psilocybin, and meditation: all three show DMN deactivation and share core mystical attributes (Stace's criteria); proposes filter/reducing valve theory where reduced brain activity opens access to expanded consciousness rather than causing hallucination [catalog ID: woollacott_2020_mystical_neural_correlates]
- Michael et al. (2025) -- Perspective article arguing NDEs are clinically actionable: ~20% cardiac arrest and ~15% ICU incidence; NDE experiencers show more post-traumatic growth and are not more likely to develop PTSD despite more intrusive thoughts; proposes incorporating NDE-C scale screening into acute care management; draws psychedelic-NDE parallels for set-and-setting optimization [catalog ID: martial_2025_nde_clinical_relevance]
- Pehlivanova, Carroll & Greyson (2022) -- Largest feature-level study to date (N=384 NDErs) of which specific NDE phenomenological elements predict fear-of-death reduction: encountering mystical beings (ρ=−0.22) and undergoing a life review (ρ=−0.22) are strongest predictors of reduced Fear of Death (Bonferroni-corrected p<0.003); cosmic unity predicts lower Death Anxiety; out-of-body disembodiment does NOT predict fear reduction, refuting Tassell-Matamua & Lindsay's (2016) hypothesis. Identifies transcendental rather than somatic NDE elements as active ingredients for lasting death-attitude transformation [catalog ID: greyson_2022_nde_fear_death]
- Greyson (2001) -- Cross-sectional survey of 194 close-brush-with-death survivors (148 NDErs, 46 non-NDErs): NDErs scored 9.0 points higher on the Impact of Event Scale (95% CI = 3.4–13.7), but only on the intrusion subscale (B = 7.59, p < 0.001), not avoidance; NDErs' scores fell 1.2 SD below clinical PTSD thresholds, suggesting NDEs produce vivid re-experiencing without pathological avoidance, consistent with a protective role of NDE positive affect [catalog ID: greyson_2001_ptsd]
- Delorme, Radin & Wahbeh (2021) -- Structured A–F evidence-grading review of nine survival-evidence categories (IONS/Bigelow essay contest): mental and physical mediumship receive B+, reincarnation B-, NDE OBE aspects B-; no category achieves A because the psi-vs-survival confound cannot be resolved; survey of 422 academics ranks a controlled veridical OBE during NDE as the most persuasive possible experiment (mean rating 14.4/50), defining the operational gold standard for what would constitute decisive pro-survival evidence [catalog ID: delorme_2021_survival_consciousness]
- Hashemi et al. (2023) -- Systematic analysis of 54 studies (465 NDErs) across multiple cultures finds a universal phenomenological core (OBEs in 35/54 studies, heightened senses in 39/54); cultural differences confined to interpretation not experiential structure; concludes NDEs are "medically inexplicable" with a "stable pattern of enhanced consciousness" [catalog ID: kondziella_2023_nde_systematic_analysis]
- Nahm et al. (2012) -- Literature review identifying 83 cases of "terminal lucidity" over 250 years — unexpected return of full mental clarity and memory shortly before death in patients with severe brain damage (tumors, strokes, Alzheimer's, schizophrenia); historical asylum data show 13% prevalence; cases involving extensive brain tissue destruction pose difficulties for prevailing neurological models of cognition [catalog ID: nahm_2012_terminal_lucidity]
- Kelly (2001) -- Systematic comparison of 74 NDE cases involving deceased-person encounters vs. 200 without (N=274, UVA collection): those closer to death significantly more likely to see deceased (76% vs 51%, χ²=6.69, p<.01); deceased-person cases associated with sudden-onset conditions (χ²=13.02, p<.025); no age difference between groups; 32% of deceased seen were emotionally neutral or never met; only 11/274 saw living persons; argues convergence of features weakens expectation/hallucination hypothesis [catalog ID: kelly_2001_nde_meeting_deceased]
- Parnia et al. (2023) -- AWARE II: largest prospective study with concurrent brain monitoring across 25 hospitals; of 567 IHCA, 53 survived, 28 interviewed, 11 (39.3%) reported consciousness; EEG showed normal cortical activity (delta, theta, alpha) emerging 35-60 min into CPR despite severe cerebral ischemia (mean rSO2=43%); however, hidden audiovisual targets produced no confirmed hits, and only 2/28 interviewed survivors had usable EEG data [catalog ID: parnia_2023_aware_ii]

**Con:**
- Blanke et al. (2002) -- Focal electrical stimulation of the right angular gyrus in a single epilepsy patient reproducibly induced OBEs at 3.5 mA (patient reported floating near ceiling viewing own body from above); lower currents produced vestibular sensations; same site induced complex somatosensory illusions; demonstrates OBEs can be artificially triggered by cortical stimulation [catalog ID: blanke_2002_stimulating_obe]
- Blanke et al. (2004) -- Systematic case series of 6 neurological patients with OBE/AS: in 5 of 6, brain damage/dysfunction localized to the temporo-parietal junction (TPJ); OBE associated with vestibular dysfunction (floating, elevation) and supine position; proposes testable theory that OBE requires dual disintegration of personal-space body schema and personal-extrapersonal vestibular space [catalog ID: blanke_2004_oobe_neurological]
- Hou et al. (2013) -- First prospective study of NDEs in severe traumatic brain injury patients (N=86, GCS<8, coma>72hrs): only 3.5% reported NDEs vs. 10-35% in cardiac arrest studies; no out-of-body experiences reported; supports 'dying brain' hypothesis that NDEs require specific neurophysiological dying processes absent in trauma-induced coma [catalog ID: hou_2013_nde_features]
- Beauregard (2012) -- While generally sympathetic, notes that current brain-imaging technology cannot definitively resolve whether NDEs occur during flat EEG or during the recovery period
- Bókkon, Mallick & Tuszynski (2013) -- Proposes that brilliant light perception in NDEs arises from bioluminescent biophoton emission during brain hypoxia/reperfusion; rat brain chemiluminescence rose from 11±15 to 231±35 counts/10s-g during hypoxia; extends to REM-dream-like visual imagery via biophotonic representations [catalog ID: bokkon_2013_nde_multidisciplinary]
- Charland-Verville et al. (2014) -- Retrospective comparison of 190 NDEs: NDE-like experiences from non-life-threatening events (n=50) were phenomenologically indistinguishable from real NDEs after coma (n=140) on both intensity (total score 17+/-7 vs 16+/-6, p=0.10) and all 16 individual Greyson NDE scale features; suggests NDEs are not dependent on actual proximity to death, challenging both survival interpretations and purely anoxia-based explanations [catalog ID: charland_verville_2014_nde_non_life_threatening]
- Martial et al. (2017) -- Text analysis of 154 NDE narratives finds no invariable temporal sequence of features; most common 4-feature sequence (OBE→Tunnel→Light→Peace) appeared in only 22% of qualifying narratives (6/27); NDE experiences are highly individual, undermining both Ring's fixed-stage model and simple neurological cascade theories [catalog ID: martial_2017_nde_features]
- Timmermann et al. (2018) -- Within-subjects placebo-controlled study administering IV DMT to 13 healthy volunteers: all scored above NDE threshold (≥7) on Greyson scale (d = 3.09 vs. placebo); DMT-induced NDE scores were comparable to matched actual NDE experiencers (d = 0.49, p = 0.089); NDE scores correlated strongly with ego dissolution (r = 0.69) and mystical experience (r = 0.90); baseline delusional ideation predicted NDE intensity. Provides pharmacological evidence that the full NDE phenomenological profile can be reproduced by a serotonergic drug [catalog ID: timmermann_2018_dmt_nde]
- Romand & Ehret (2023) -- Comprehensive review of neuro-functional NDE models: evaluates evidence from drugs (ketamine, DMT), epilepsy, brain stimulation, anesthesia awareness, and ischemic stress including ~1,000 fighter pilot G-LOC episodes; finds NDE themes collectively reproduced across all approaches; localizes OBEs to the temporo-parietal junction; concludes NDEs are hallucination-like phenomena from brains in altered states of consciousness [catalog ID: palmieri_2022_neurofunctional_nde]
- Borjigin et al. (2013) -- First systematic EEG study of dying mammalian brain: cardiac arrest in 9/9 rats triggered a ~30-second surge of globally coherent gamma oscillations with feedback connectivity 8× waking levels (P < 0.0001) and cross-frequency coupling patterns consistent with conscious processing; replicated with CO2 asphyxiation; provides neurobiological framework for NDE phenomenology without requiring non-corporeal consciousness [catalog ID: borjigin_2013_surge_dying]
- Mobbs & Watt (2011) -- "There Is Nothing Paranormal About Near-Death Experiences" maps each NDE feature to a neuroscientific mechanism: tunnel vision to retinal ischemia, OBE to TPJ disruption (Blanke et al. 2002), encounters with deceased to dopaminergic hallucinations, feeling of being dead to Cotard syndrome; explicitly critiqued by Facco & Agrillo (2012) for offering analogies without identity [catalog ID: mobbs_2011_nothing_paranormal]
- Nichols (2017) -- Pharmacological debunking of the DMT-pineal hypothesis: the adult pineal produces only ~30 µg/day melatonin, making the ~25 mg DMT needed for psychoactive effects implausible by ~1000-fold; INMT mRNA is very low in adult brain; no credible evidence for DMT accumulation in neurons; proposes dynorphin/kappa-opioid activation and massive neurotransmitter surges during asphyxia (NE >30×, 5-HT >20×, DA >12×) as better-supported explanations for near-death altered states [catalog ID: nichols_2017_endogenous_dmt]

**Current status:** NDEs remain one of the most challenging phenomena for both sides of the debate. The field began with retrospective qualitative studies (Moody 1977) that established the core phenomenology and demonstrated the phenomenon's widespread occurrence across independent investigators. The prospective studies (van Lommel 2001, Parnia 2014) represent serious attempts to capture NDEs as they occur rather than relying on retrospective reports, and the AWARE study's design (with hidden visual targets in hospital rooms) was specifically intended to test veridical out-of-body perception. However, the hidden targets have not produced confirmed hits at scale. The memory research (Thonnard 2013, Moore & Greyson 2017) adds a new dimension by showing NDEs have distinctive memory characteristics — with Moore & Greyson's large-sample replication (N=122, η²=0.486) providing the strongest quantitative evidence that NDE memories are phenomenologically richer than real-event memories — but does not settle whether this reflects a genuinely non-cerebral experience or an unusual neurological state. Separately, Cassol et al. (2018) applied rigorous qualitative thematic analysis to NDE narratives, validating the Greyson Scale's core features while identifying three additional themes, strengthening the case that NDEs have a consistent phenomenological structure. The discovery that NDEs occur in non-life-threatening circumstances (Charland-Verville 2014) complicates the survival interpretation. On the Con side, Mobbs & Watt (2011) provide the most concise skeptical account, mapping each NDE feature to a known neuroscientific mechanism, but Facco & Agrillo (2012) — which was reviewed and approved by Mobbs — systematically argue these are analogies without identity. On the Pro side, Long & Holden (2007) dismantle the Nelson et al. (2006) REM-intrusion hypothesis on methodological grounds, though the original Nelson paper is not yet in the library. Borjigin et al. (2013) provided a critical piece of the mechanistic puzzle by demonstrating that the dying rat brain generates highly organized gamma oscillations — with coherence and directed connectivity exceeding waking levels — offering the first direct neurophysiological evidence for how the brain could generate vivid conscious experience during cardiac arrest. AWARE II (Parnia et al. 2023) represents the culmination of this trend toward larger prospective studies with concurrent brain monitoring, but its results are mixed: EEG data suggest cortical activity persists far longer than expected during cardiac arrest, yet the hidden-target paradigm again failed to produce confirmed hits, the sample was reduced by COVID-19, and the disconnect between EEG data and subjective reports (only 2 of 28 interviewed survivors had EEG data, neither among NDE reporters) means the central question remains unresolved. Definitive evidence remains elusive.
