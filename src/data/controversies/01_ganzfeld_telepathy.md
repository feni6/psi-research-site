# 1. Does the ganzfeld produce replicable evidence for telepathy?

The ganzfeld protocol -- in which a "receiver" in sensory deprivation attempts to identify a target being viewed by a remote "sender" -- is the single most debated experimental paradigm in parapsychology. The central question is whether the hit rate (typically reported around 32% against a 25% chance baseline) reflects genuine anomalous information transfer or persistent methodological and statistical artifacts.

**Pro:**
- Bem & Honorton (1994) -- Autoganzfeld experiments yield 32% hit rate (z=2.89, p=.002, 329 sessions) with automated computer-controlled protocols; reviews 28 prior ganzfeld studies (composite z=6.60); dynamic targets 37% vs. static 27%; Juilliard students hit at 50%; published in *Psychological Bulletin* [catalog ID: bem_1994_does]
- Storm, Tressoldi & Di Risio (2010) -- Meta-analysis of free-response studies (1992-2008) finds significant cumulative effect [catalog ID: storm_2010_metaanalysis]
- Storm & Tressoldi (2020) -- 10-year update meta-analysis (2009-2018): 43 new studies combined with 1992-2008 databases yield k=108 total; ganzfeld (k=38) ES=0.133, 95% CI [0.071, 0.194], Stouffer Z=5.95, p=1.37x10^-9; no decline across 44 years; Bayesian analysis confirms; selected participants outperform unselected only in ganzfeld [catalog ID: storm_2020_metaanalysis]
- Tressoldi, Storm & Radin (2010) -- Narrative review synthesizing all six ganzfeld meta-analyses (108 studies, 4,196 trials): overall hit rate 31.5% vs. 25% chance, π = 0.58 (95% CI .56–.60, z = 9.9, p = 1.0 × 10⁻¹¹); argues quantum-inspired cognitive models provide a natural theoretical framework for nonlocal information transfer [catalog ID: tressoldi_2010_extrasensory]
- Storm & Ertel (2001) -- Rebuttal to Milton & Wiseman (1999); unified 79 ganzfeld/autoganzfeld studies yielding ES = 0.138, Z = 5.66, p = 7.78 × 10⁻⁹; 31% hit rate vs. 25% MCE; significant bidirectional psi in all databases including M&W's alone [catalog ID: storm_2001_does]
- Bem, Palmer & Broughton (2001) -- Meta-analysis of 40 post-1987 ganzfeld studies: 10 new studies yield 36.7% hit rate (Z=3.97, p=3.5×10⁻⁵); all 40 combined yield 30.1% (Z=2.59, p=.0048). Three independent raters rated protocol adherence; standardness significantly correlated with effect size (r=.31, p=.024). Standard replications (n=29) achieved 31.2% hit rate (ES=.096, Z=3.49, p=.0002), within confidence intervals of earlier studies; non-standard (n=9) achieved 24.0% (ES=−.10, ns). Demonstrates protocol adherence moderates effect sizes, explaining Milton-Wiseman null results [catalog ID: bem_palmer_broughton_2001_updating_ganzfeld]
- Storm et al. (2017) -- Meta-analysis of 50 dream-ESP studies (1966-2016) yields ES=0.20, Stouffer Z=5.32, p=5.19×10^-8; both Maimonides and non-MDL studies significant [catalog ID: storm_2017_correspondence]
- Child (1985) -- Independent reanalysis of Maimonides dream ESP data in *American Psychologist*: hits exceeded misses on 15 of 15 segments (sign test p < .0001); combined p < .000002 for outside judges on segments free of nonindependence; demonstrated five psychology textbooks either ignored or falsified the experimental procedures [catalog ID: child_1985_psychology_anomalous]
- Grinberg-Zylberbaum et al. (1994) -- Foundational 'transferred potential' study: 7 pairs meditated together then separated into Faraday chambers 14.5 m apart; ~25% of pairs showed transferred potentials in unstimulated subject morphologically similar to partner's evoked potentials, with correlations r = 0.70-0.93 (p < 0.005). Controls showed no effect. NOTE: Not a ganzfeld study — this is the founding EEG brain-to-brain correlation experiment [catalog ID: grinberg_zylberbaum_1994_transferred_potential]
- Standish et al. (2003) -- First fMRI demonstration of distant brain correlations: receiver in Faraday-shielded MRI scanner showed significant BOLD activation (P < .001) in visual cortex areas 18-19 while sender viewed flickering checkerboard 10 m away; N=2 (one colleague pair); effect not observed when roles reversed. NOTE: Not a ganzfeld study — fMRI correlated brain signals case report [catalog ID: standish_2003_evidence]
- Standish et al. (2004) -- Largest EEG correlation study (N=60, 30 pairs): 5 of 60 subjects (8.3%) showed significant brain activation (p<0.01) during partner's flicker condition; Stouffer z=-3.28 (p=0.0005) across all subjects; static control non-significant (z=0.35, p=0.64); 1 of 4 retested pairs replicated effect. Rooms not EM-shielded deliberately to avoid blocking potential phenomenon. NOTE: Not a ganzfeld study — EEG brain-to-brain correlation experiment [catalog ID: standish_2004_electroencephalographic]
- Radin (2004) -- Extends Grinberg-Zylberbaum's transferred-potential paradigm: 13 pairs had EEGs recorded while the sender was stimulated by receiver's live video image; receiver in double steel-walled EM-shielded chamber 20 m away. S-R EEG correlation r = 0.20, z = 3.32, p = 0.0005; equipment-only controls showed no artifact (r = -0.03); dose-response: stronger sender ERPs predicted larger receiver responses (z = 3.15, p = 0.0008). Triple-blind epoch timing. NOTE: Not a ganzfeld study — EEG brain-to-brain correlation experiment [catalog ID: radin_2004_event]
- Richards et al. (2005) -- fMRI and EEG study of one pre-selected pair: subject DW showed significant BOLD activation in visual cortex BA 17/18/19 correlated with isolated partner's flickering stimulus in both trials (p < 0.017, Bonferroni corrected); subject CW replicated in trial 2; EEG confirmed correlated alpha-power changes (χ² = 455.4, p < .0001). However, N=2 with extreme pre-selection (1 pair from 30). NOTE: This is NOT a ganzfeld study — it is a correlated brain signals experiment and belongs primarily in Controversy #14 [catalog ID: richards_2005_replicable]
- Tressoldi et al. (2011) -- Forced-choice telepathy without sensory deprivation: Exp1 (N=40) Chinese ideogram identification with distant helper yielded 10.3% above MCE (ES=0.44, BF10=23.8); Exp2 (N=70) simpler sun/moon task yielded 8.8% above MCE (ES=0.27, BF10=2.8); results comparable to ganzfeld hit rates without altered states. Absorption did not moderate. NOTE: This paper was originally miscataloged as a 2014 EEG study [catalog ID: tressoldi_2011_mental_connection]
- Tressoldi (2011) -- Bayesian meta-analysis of ganzfeld database yields BF=18.8 million in favor of H1, arguing this meets "extraordinary evidence" standard [catalog ID: tressoldi_2011_extraordinary_claims]
- Milton (1997) -- Meta-analysis of 78 non-ganzfeld free-response ESP studies (1964–1992): mean ES = 0.16, Stouffer Z = 5.72, p < 5.4 × 10⁻⁹ across 2,682 trials; file-drawer of 866 null studies needed; quality-weighted telepathy z = 3.77 (p < 10⁻⁴); no flaw-effect correlation but 96% of studies lacked prespecified outcome measures; establishes the non-ganzfeld baseline that Storm et al. (2010) later compared against [catalog ID: milton_1997_free_response]
- Utts (1991) -- Survey of four psi meta-analyses in *Statistical Science* concludes autoganzfeld hit rate of 34.4% (p=0.00005, h=0.20) constitutes an anomalous effect needing explanation; includes commentary from six distinguished statisticians including Diaconis, Mosteller, and Berger [catalog ID: utts_1991_replication_meta_analysis]
- Hyman & Honorton (1986) -- Joint communiqué by the leading skeptic and leading proponent replacing a planned exchange. Both authors agree the ganzfeld database shows "an overall significant effect that cannot reasonably be explained by selective reporting or multiple analysis." Issues six prescriptive methodological recommendations (sensory leakage control, randomization documentation, judging/feedback procedures, pre-specified Bonferroni corrections, file-drawer registration, statistical reporting). Calls for experiments planned with future meta-analysis in mind. Directly shaped the AutoGanzfeld program design [catalog ID: hyman_honorton_1986_joint_communique]
- Baptista, Derakhshani & Tressoldi (2014) -- Comprehensive review of ganzfeld evidence including new analyses of Storm et al. (2010) database; selected participants produce 40.1% hit rate vs. 27.3% unselected (Fisher's exact p < .0001); higher-quality studies yield larger effect sizes (r = .37, p = .045), contradicting skeptical predictions; no reliable decline effects; file-drawer analysis using KPU + PRL datasets (524 trials, 32.8% HR, p = 5.91 × 10⁻⁸) with no excluded studies [catalog ID: baptista_2015_explicit_anomalous_cognition]
- Tressoldi & Storm (2024) -- Stage 2 Registered Report meta-analysis of 78 ganzfeld studies (113 effect sizes, 1974-2020): overall ES = 0.08 (95% CI [0.04, 0.12]; BF₁₀ = 89.5); passes 4 publication bias tests (3PSM, p-uniform*, RoBMA, Mathur-VanderWeele); no decline over time (meta-regression slope = 0.0012, p = 0.53); effect stable since 1997; selected participants ES = 0.13 vs. 0.04 non-selected; Type 3 (telepathy) tasks ES = 0.08 vs. Type 2 (clairvoyance) ES = 0.04 [catalog ID: tressoldi_2024_ganzfeld_meta]

**Con:**
- Milton & Wiseman (1999) -- Meta-analysis of post-1987 ganzfeld studies finds no significant effect, suggesting earlier results were driven by methodological flaws [catalog ID: milton_1999_does]
- Hyman (1994) -- Reanalysis of autoganzfeld data finds hit rate strongly correlated with target occurrence frequency (Spearman r = .83, p = .013) and a significant target-occurrence × experimenter-prompting interaction (chi-squared = 14.702, p = .0001); argues randomization testing was inadequate and positive results may reflect artifact [catalog ID: hyman_1994_anomaly]
- Hyman (1996) -- Commissioned evaluation of SRI/SAIC program argues autoganzfeld data show key inconsistencies: static targets yielded zero effect size despite comprising the bulk of the original ganzfeld database; all significant hitting occurred only on second or later target occurrences; extroversion-psi correlation reversed in Edinburgh replication; similarity in average effect sizes across labs is meaningless given extreme heterogeneity [catalog ID: hyman_1996_evaluation]
- Hyman (2010) -- Commentary on Storm et al.'s ganzfeld meta-analysis demonstrating that apparent consistency is manufactured by outlier removal, combining heterogeneous databases, and confusing failure to reject null with proving homogeneity; shows Autoganzfeld II (N=209, meeting all Storm et al. criteria) yielded chance-level hit rates of 25.8%; introduces the N-rays analogy — 180 successful N-ray experiments would yield a significant meta-analytic effect size for a nonexistent phenomenon [catalog ID: hyman_2010_meta_analysis_conceals]
- Ioannidis (2005) -- The mathematical framework explaining why small-sample ganzfeld studies (typically n < 50 per lab) with small effects (~32% hit rate vs 25% chance) are likely false positives; illustrates Corollary 1 (small studies) and Corollary 4 (flexible protocols) [catalog ID: ioannidis_2005_false]
- Moulton & Kosslyn (2008) -- Harvard fMRI study with 16 sender-receiver pairs (including twins, couples): behavioral guessing at exact chance (50.0%, 3,687 trials), no significant brain activation differences between psi and non-psi stimuli at group level; control arousal contrast confirmed method sensitivity (p = 9.35 x 10^-21); authors call results "strongest evidence yet obtained against" psi [catalog ID: moulton_2008_using]
- Bierman, Spottiswoode & Bijl (2016) -- Monte Carlo simulation of seven QRPs applied to 78 post-1985 ganzfeld studies: with realistic QRP prevalence rates, simulations account for ~60% of the reported 6% hit-rate excess; the residual ~2% excess remains significant (p=0.003) but the widely cited p~10⁻¹⁶ is shown to be grossly inflated by QRPs [catalog ID: bierman_2016_questionable_practices]
- Rouder, Morey & Province (2013) -- Bayesian reanalysis of Storm et al.'s (2010) 67 free-response studies: full dataset yields BF₁₀ ≈ 6 billion, but manually randomized studies show significantly higher hit rates (BF ≈ 6,350 for the difference); excluding these and including omitted null conditions reduces evidence to BF₁₀ ≈ 32–328, argued unpersuasive given absence of mechanism [catalog ID: rouder_2013_bayes_factor_esp]

**Current status:** The ganzfeld remains one of parapsychology's most-replicated paradigms, but the debate turns on study inclusion criteria and quality thresholds. Pro-psi meta-analyses (Storm 2010, 2020) find a robust effect across 108 studies spanning 1992-2018 with no decline over 44 years (ES~0.13 for ganzfeld); critical meta-analyses (Milton & Wiseman 1999) argue the effect vanishes when stricter quality filters are applied, and Rouder et al. (2013) attribute much of the effect to manually randomized studies. The impasse reflects deeper disagreements about what counts as an adequate control in this domain. New work using neuroimaging (Moulton & Kosslyn 2008) has generally not supported the effect, while behavioral ganzfeld studies continue to produce above-chance hit rates, particularly with selected participants. The 2024 Tressoldi & Storm registered-report meta-analysis adds pre-registered rigor to the pro-psi position.
