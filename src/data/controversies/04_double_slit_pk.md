# 4. Does consciousness influence quantum systems (double-slit PK)?

A series of experiments, primarily from Dean Radin's lab at IONS, claims that human conscious observation or intention can influence the interference pattern in a double-slit optical system -- a quantum-level psychokinesis (PK) effect. If real, this would link consciousness directly to quantum measurement processes.

**Pro:**
- Tiller (1990) -- Gas discharge cell operated below breakdown voltage at Stanford; ~50 subjects tested over 3 years (1977-1979) in several thousand tests; nearly all produced enhanced electron microavalanche counting rates (WBH/WOH ratio averaging ~2×10⁴:1). Faraday cage shielding did not block the effect; mental focus alone (without hand proximity) produced enhancement; hands present with attention diverted to arithmetic produced zero enhancement. No conventional energy source could reproduce the effect. An early instrumentation approach to mind-matter interaction distinct from RNG-based paradigms [catalog ID: tiller_1990_gas_discharge]
- Schmidt (1987) -- Foundational theoretical-experimental paper articulating the 'weak violation' hypothesis (PK affects only quantum-indeterminate outcomes) and 'equivalence' hypothesis (all random generators equally susceptible). Reviews his PK experiments: Exp1 50.9% hits (z=3.3, 1000:1, 32,768 bits); Exp2 52.4% (z=5.3, 10^7:1, 12,800 bits); pre-recorded PK: 54.6% of 832 blocks successful with four replications (z=3.1, 4.2, 2.0, 2.7); observer-inhibition: PK succeeded (z=3.1) only when subject was first to observe pre-recorded events, blocked (z=-0.7) when control observed first. Proposes PK acts at quantum state collapse, directly linking consciousness to quantum measurement [catalog ID: schmidt_1987_strange]
- Radin et al. (2012) -- "Consciousness and the Double-Slit Interference Pattern" reports statistically significant shifts in fringe visibility correlated with observer intention [catalog ID: radin_2012_consciousness]
- *[Radin et al. (2019) — referenced as `radin_2019_double_slit` in this controversy but NOT in the catalog — a phantom entry. Likely a Physics Essays paper in the double-slit series. Track down and add to library to fix this broken reference.]*
- Radin, Michel & Delorme (2016) -- Online double-slit experiment with 1,479 participants from 77 countries contributing 2,985 sessions over 2013-2014. Combined analysis: z = 5.72, p = 1.05 x 10^-8; robot control sessions showed z = 0.17. Accidental feedback reversal between years provided evidence for active intentional steering rather than passive observation [catalog ID: radin_2016_double_slit]
- Radin & Delorme (2022) -- Exploratory reanalysis of a two-year online double-slit experiment (8,294 sessions: 2,825 human, 5,469 robot). The original directional hypothesis was not confirmed by Tremblay (2019/2021). A new variance-based (bidirectional) analysis, motivated by the hypothesis that mind-wandering causes psychophysical effects to fluctuate, found significant variance differences: 2013 data z = 4.16 (p = .00002), 2014 held-out replication z = 3.14 (p = .0008), combined Stouffer Z = 5.57 (p = 1.3 × 10⁻⁸). Environmental checks (laser power, time of day, session distribution) all negative. Published in *Journal of Anomalous Experience and Cognition* [catalog ID: radin_2022_psychophysical_effects_double_slit]
- Radin & Anastasia (2022) -- Three experiments testing directional intention on ionized plasma streams in a sealed EM-shielded chamber (optical-fibre USB isolation). NOTE: this is a *plasma globe* paradigm, not a double-slit system — a different physical target testing the same theoretical claim. Most rigorous experiment (N=13): directional intentional aims produced significantly different spatial illumination patterns (z=5.42, p=6×10⁻⁸, ES=1.00 for two different plasma balls) [catalog ID: radin_2022_psychophysical]
- Radin, Wahbeh, Michel & Delorme (2021) -- Previously unreported 2012-2013 double-slit experiment (N=25, 250 sessions in shielded chamber at IONS). The planned analysis found no significant effect after FDR adjustment. Two exploratory post hoc analyses were positive: simplified spectral metric z = −2.829 (p = 0.002), and 12-element causal model Spearman ρ = 0.873 (p = 0.0003). Fringe visibility: 7/22 fringes above 2.3σ after FDR. Sham sessions uniformly null. Temperature and vibration controls negative. No real-time feedback was provided. 8-year publication delay due to funder (Walleczek) withholding request [catalog ID: radin_2021_psychophysical]
- Radin, Wahbeh, Michel & Delorme (2020) -- Commentary rebutting Walleczek & von Stillfried (2019): argues their false-positive claim was invalid because eight non-overlapping comparisons required multiple-comparison correction (p of at least one false positive = 34%); after FDR adjustment none of eight mean comparisons survived, but a pre-planned variance comparison (unreported by WS) remained significant; cumulative evidence across 28 double-slit experiments by four groups yields binomial p < 10⁻⁷ [catalog ID: radin_2020_commentary_ds_false_positive]
- Radin (2025) -- Preregistered 47-participant diffraction grating experiment with custom-built at-home interferometers; three primary hypotheses not supported overall, but exploratory trend analysis found progressive interference decline at the observed sensor vs. simultaneous unobserved control (t = 9.24, p = 5.9 × 10⁻¹⁴); magicians outperformed meditators (z = 2.39, p = 0.008); 212 no-observer control sessions null [catalog ID: radin_2025_observer]
- Radin (2008) -- "Testing Nonlocal Observation as a Source of Intuitive Knowledge" — pilot Michelson interferometer experiment in double-steel-walled shielded chamber; 18 sessions with 10 participants; overall z = −2.82 (p = .002); effect driven entirely by experienced meditators (z = −4.28, p = 9.4 × 10⁻⁶); nonmeditators null (z = −0.29); 15 control sessions null (z = −1.50, p = .93). The direct precursor to Radin's 2012–2025 double-slit series [catalog ID: radin_2008_nonlocal_observation]
- Kauffman & Radin (2023) -- Proposes a quantum-mind theoretical framework (Res potentia / Res extensa) that predicts consciousness-mediated effects on physical systems; reviews the double-slit series (30 experiments by 5 labs, cumulative p < 4 × 10⁻¹¹) as key supporting evidence; published in *BioSystems* [catalog ID: kauffman_2023_quantum]
- Freedman, Jeffers, Saeger et al. (2003) -- "Effects of Frontal Lobe Lesions on Intentionality and Random Physical Phenomena" tests PEAR claims using patients with frontal lobe damage (hypothesized to have reduced self-awareness facilitating psi). Left frontal patient showed significant REG deviation (p=0.0015, replicated p=0.0115), lateralized contralateral to lesion. No effects in bilateral frontal, right frontal, or normal subjects. Uses experimental controls addressing PEAR methodology concerns [catalog ID: freedman_2003_effects]
- Freedman, Binns, Gao et al. (2018) -- "Mind-Matter Interactions and the Frontal Lobes of the Brain" proposes neurobiological model where frontal lobes (medial middle frontal region, Brodmann areas 9, 10, 32) act as psi-inhibitory filter via self-awareness mechanisms. Two participants with frontal lobe damage showed significant REG deviations (effect sizes 4-15x normal), lateralized contralateral to lesion. Suggests frontal damage creates 'psi-enriched' population [catalog ID: freedman_2018_mindmatter]
- Bierman (2003) -- Conceptual replication of Hall et al. (1977) testing whether conscious observation collapses the quantum wave-packet. EEG recorded from 30 final observers while a pre-observer randomly saw (or did not see) the same radioactive decay event 1 second earlier. Three of 10 peak comparisons significant; overall exact binomial p=0.0115. Significant effects appeared in early pre-conscious EEG components (<100ms), consistent with the subjective reduction hypothesis [catalog ID: bierman_2003_does]

- Radin, Nelson, Dobyns & Houtkooper (2006) -- Point-by-point rebuttal of Bösch et al. (2006) in *Psychological Bulletin*; argues the core assumption that PK effect size is independent of sample size is incorrect for experiments where psychological context matters; demonstrates the four largest PEAR studies (4.54×10^11 bits, 320x more data than all other studies combined) refute the constant per-bit model; shows Bösch et al.'s Monte Carlo simulation has a built-in small-study effect; survey of researchers found only ~59 unreported experiments vs. the model's 1,544 [catalog ID: radin_2006_reexamining]
- Kugel (2011) -- "A Faulty PK Meta-Analysis" provides the most detailed critique of BSB (2006): demonstrates BSB included ~40 ESP studies in their PK database, fabricated z-scores from misinterpreted statistics, used unrelated hardware tests as controls, and that the entire negative result (z = −3.67) comes from three PEAR high-speed RNG studies with potentially malfunctioning equipment; without those three studies z = +3.59. Kugel's critique is especially credible because he demonstrates coding errors using his own experimental data [catalog ID: kugel_2011_faulty_pk_meta]

**Con:**
- Jahn et al. (2000) -- Three-laboratory consortium (PEAR/Princeton, FAMMI/Freiburg, GARP/Giessen) attempted to replicate PEAR's 12-year REG database using identical PortREG equipment and tripolar protocol; 227 operators generated 750 sessions (~2.25 million 200-bit trials). Primary criterion — matching PEAR's prior HI-LO mean shift of delta=0.0208, Z=3.809 — failed by an order of magnitude (combined Z=0.596, delta=0.0034). Authors note structural anomalies (reduced trial-level standard deviations, secondary-parameter dependencies, composite p=0.001–0.002) but the primary effect was not replicated; the most rigorous multi-lab test of the PEAR/REG paradigm [catalog ID: jahn_2000_replication]
- Bösch, Steinkamp & Boller (2006) -- Meta-analysis of 380 RNG-PK studies (1959-2004) finds a significant but extremely small overall effect (REM: π = .500286, z = 4.08 excl. 3 outliers), but a strong inverse relationship between sample size and effect size (small-study effect) and extreme heterogeneity (Q = 1508.56); Monte Carlo simulation shows publication bias could account for all findings; concludes "not proven" [catalog ID: bosch_2006_examining]
- Reber & Alcock (2019) -- "Searching for the Impossible" argues that psi effects, including quantum-mind interactions, violate fundamental physical principles and positive results must reflect methodological error [catalog ID: reber_2019_searching]
- Maier, Dechamps & Pflitsch (2018) -- Online experiment with 12,571 participants using Bayesian sequential testing found strong evidence for the null hypothesis (BF01=10.07; M=50.02% vs. 50% chance) with a Quantis quantum RNG. No personality moderators significant. Exploratory dampened-oscillation analysis found higher oscillation frequency in human data vs. simulated data, but this was post hoc [catalog ID: maier_2018_intentional_observer]
- Maier & Dechamps (2018) -- "Observer Effects on Quantum Randomness: Testing Micro-Psychokinetic Effects of Smokers on Addiction-Related Stimuli" tested unconscious desire (cigarette addiction) as moderator in 254 participants. Study 1 found strong evidence for micro-Pk in smokers (BF10=66.06, M=196.7 vs 200 chance). Pre-registered Study 2 replication (N=395) failed completely (BF01=11.07). Combined analysis revealed appearance-then-decline pattern in smokers only, interpreted as dampened harmonic oscillation per von Lucadou's MPI. Critical paper for understanding replication failures in micro-PK [catalog ID: maier_dechamps_2018_smoker_micro_pk]
- Maier & Dechamps (2020) -- Another pre-registered mind-matter study reporting null results [catalog ID: maier_2020_preregistered]
- Boesch (2019) -- Online RNG experiment with large sample finds no psychokinetic effect
- Ibison & Jeffers (1998) -- Two-site Young's double-slit experiment (York University: 74 series; Princeton PEAR: 20 series). York result: Z = −0.481 (null), though secondary analysis showed variance inflation (σ=1.185, χ²=102.4, p=0.013) and excess negative Z-scores (9 vs. 3.7 expected, p=0.011). Princeton result: Z = 1.654, marginally significant (p≈0.049). Post-hoc analysis revealed the apparatus noise was thermal detector dark noise, not quantum photon granularity, making the device functionally equivalent to a random event generator and invalidating the quantum observer framing [catalog ID: ibison_jeffers_1998_double_slit]
- Alcock (2003) -- "Give the Null Hypothesis a Chance" discusses Jeffers' null double-slit experiments at York University (now cataloged as ibison_jeffers_1998_double_slit) as evidence that consciousness does not influence quantum systems; argues the double-slit PK paradigm exemplifies psi research's reliance on post hoc statistical discovery and unfalsifiable hypotheses [catalog ID: alcock_2003_give]
- Walleczek & von Stillfried (2019) -- Commissioned conceptual replication of the Radin DS-experiment using the Advanced Meta-Experimental Protocol (AMP): 10,000 test trials with pre-specified blinded analysis, performed by Radin himself. The true-experiment failed to replicate the original results (0% match). The sham-experiment (no test subjects) produced a significant false-positive in the predicted test category (p = 0.021, σ = −2.02, N = 1,250), with false-positive effect size ~0.01% — within an order of magnitude of the claimed 0.001% consciousness effect. False-positive detection rate: 50% [catalog ID: walleczek_2019_false_positive_amp]
- Walleczek & von Stillfried (2020) -- Response commentary documenting two instances of HARKing by Radin et al. in their defense of the double-slit experiment: a chi-square test falsely claimed as pre-specified was actually post-hoc, and a call for multiple-testing correction abandoned the planned predictive single-testing strategy after unblinding. Three independent sources (Guerrer 2019, Tremblay 2019, Walleczek & von Stillfried 2019) converge on the conclusion that the DS-experiment is prone to false discoveries [catalog ID: walleczek_2020_false_positive_radin]
- Pitkänen (2017) -- Theoretical commentary raising experimenter effect concern: if mental intention can influence physical systems, experimenters' subconscious desires for positive results could produce false positives; proposes Topological Geometrodynamics (TGD) framework as alternative explanatory model involving magnetic flux tubes as correlates of attention [catalog ID: pitkanen_2017_radin_double_slit_critique]
- Tremblay (2019) -- Independent re-analysis of Radin et al.'s 2-year double-slit dataset (8,655 sessions) identifies erroneous trimming procedure (trimming before bootstrapping instead of after) that produces uncontrolled false positives and underestimates p-values by ~5 orders of magnitude. Re-analysis with proper statistics finds shifts in predicted direction but not significant (p>0.05 after Holm-Bonferroni correction across 26 time lags, 19 fringes). Original claim of 5.72σ evidence (p=1.05×10⁻⁸) not supported; actual evidence much weaker (p~10⁻³ under original analytical choices). Published in PLoS ONE with open data [catalog ID: tremblay_2019_reanalysis_double_slit]

**Current status:** The double-slit PK paradigm is one of the most provocative claims in psi research because it directly implicates consciousness in quantum mechanics. Radin's lab has published a steady series of results spanning over a decade (2012-2025), though the 2021 report is notable for having a null planned analysis with only exploratory post hoc analyses showing positive results. Independent pre-registered replications outside IONS (Maier 2018, 2020) have generally found null results. The Walleczek & von Stillfried (2019, 2020) AMP-based replication found a significant false-positive effect in a control condition but null results for Radin's predicted true-positive conditions, and documented HARKing in Radin et al.'s response -- raising concerns about the reliability of the entire paradigm's statistical claims. Tremblay (2019) independently re-analyzed Radin's 2-year dataset and identified a critical statistical error (trimming before bootstrapping) that inflated significance by ~5 orders of magnitude (claimed p=10⁻⁸, actual p>0.05 after proper correction). Critics point to the lack of successful independent replication, the reliance on post hoc metrics, Jeffers' null results, and now documented statistical errors and false positives. Proponents counter that the protocol requires specific expertise and that Radin's series shows internal consistency across multiple metrics. The broader mind-matter meta-analysis (Bosch 2006) does not specifically address the double-slit paradigm but casts doubt on the wider class of PK effects. This controversy remains unresolved and is likely to intensify as more labs attempt the protocol.
