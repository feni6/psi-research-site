# 3. Is presentiment a real physiological effect?

Presentiment (or "predictive anticipatory activity") refers to the claim that the human body shows measurable physiological responses -- changes in skin conductance, heart rate, pupil dilation, or brain activity -- several seconds before a randomly selected future stimulus, as if anticipating events that have not yet been determined.

**Pro:**
- Radin (1997) -- The foundational presentiment paper: four experiments with 31 participants viewing 1,060 randomly selected photos showed clear orienting pre-sponses in EDA peaking at 4 SE difference one second before stimulus display (permutation p = .008); combined Stouffer z ≈ 5.0 across EDA, HR, and BVP; established the experimental paradigm used in all subsequent presentiment research [catalog ID: radin_1997_unconscious_presentiment]
- Mossbridge, Tressoldi & Utts (2012) -- Meta-analysis of 26 presentiment studies finds an overall effect size of d = 0.21 [catalog ID: mossbridge_2012_predictive]
- Radin (2004) -- Electrodermal presentiment experiments showing anticipatory skin conductance responses [catalog ID: radin_2004_electrodermal]
- Bierman & Scholte (2002) -- One of the earliest fMRI studies of presentiment: 10 subjects scanned while viewing randomly presented emotional and neutral pictures; single-subject erotic vs neutral anticipatory BOLD td=2.89 (p<0.01, 0.203% difference at -3s); pooled female erotic td=1.75, violent td=1.99; pooled male erotic td=2.10 (all p<0.05); gender-specific anticipation patterns (females before both erotic and violent, males only before erotic); authors acknowledge results are exploratory and require replication with pre-specified ROIs [catalog ID: bierman_2002_fmri_presentiment]
- Radin (2011) -- "Predicting the Unpredictable" reviews 75 years of experimental evidence for anticipatory effects [catalog ID: radin_2011_predicting]
- Radin, Vieten, Michel & Delorme (2011) -- EEG presentiment in 8 advanced nondual meditators (≥3,000 hours) vs. 8 matched controls: 0/32 channels significant in controls, 5/32 in meditators (P<.05, FDR corrected); between-group comparison before audio tones: 15/32 channels significant (8 at P<.005), primarily occipital/parietal. Free-running task (random ISI) showed stronger effects, ruling out anticipatory strategies. Replicated earlier Radin & Lobach (2007) SCP findings [catalog ID: radin_2011_electrocortical]
- McCraty, Atkinson & Bradley (2004) -- First study to measure cardiac prestimulus response: heart rate deceleration significantly greater before future emotional vs. calm IAPS pictures (zpre = -3.19, p = 0.001, N=26) in counterbalanced crossover design; skin conductance showed no prestimulus effect in this meditator sample [catalog ID: mccraty_2004_electrophysiological]
- Mossbridge et al. (2014) -- "Predicting the Unpredictable: Critical Analysis and Practical Implications" refines the meta-analytic framework and addresses criticisms [catalog ID: mossbridge_2014_predicting]
- Mossbridge et al. (2015) -- Point-by-point response to Schwarzkopf's six objections: simulation of 1000 experiments demonstrates ~92% of presentiment effects cannot be explained by expectation bias (r = -0.16, p > 0.524); argues stimulus ratio imbalance biases against the PAA hypothesis; defends baseline correction methodology [catalog ID: mossbridge_2015_we_did_see_coming]
- Duggan & Tressoldi (2018) -- Meta-analysis update of Mossbridge et al. (2012): 18 new studies (26 experiments, 34 effect sizes) from 2008-2017 yield overall g = 0.29 (95% CI [0.19, 0.38], p = 8×10⁻⁶); peer-reviewed studies g = 0.38, non-peer-reviewed g = 0.22; Copas model finds no publication bias; sensitivity analysis excluding Mossbridge/Tressoldi still significant (g = 0.23) [catalog ID: duggan_2018_paa_update]
- Radin (2023) -- "Sentiment and Presentiment in Twitter" extends the presentiment paradigm from individual physiology to population-scale collective mood: analysis of 13 years of Twitter sentiment in 10 languages finds a significant negative slope in collective mood from 14 to 2 days before unpredictable negative world events (Stouffer z = -3.087, p = 0.001), with independent replication across 9 non-English languages (z = -3.229, p = 0.0006) [catalog ID: radin_2023_sentiment]
- Radin & Lobach (2007) -- Slow cortical potentials at Oz differentiate before randomly determined light flash vs. no-flash (zpre=2.72, p=0.007 in females, N=20); sham brain control negative; proposes retrocausal component to placebo effect [catalog ID: radin_2007_toward]
- Tressoldi et al. (2011) -- Pupil dilation predicts alerting sounds at 60.3% accuracy (z = 5.76, p = 4.2 × 10⁻⁹, BF₁₀ = 3,225, N=80) using an automated prediction algorithm; extends the presentiment paradigm from skin conductance and heart rate to a new physiological measure [catalog ID: tressoldi_2011_your]
- Spottiswoode & May (2003) -- Audio startle presentiment study using 97-dB white noise instead of IAPS pictures to eliminate idiosyncratic responses: N=125 first-time participants, Z=3.27, ES_per_stimulus=0.0901+/-0.0275, p=5.4x10^-4; true RNG sampled AFTER prestimulus data recorded; effect absent in [-6,-3)s window (t_diff=2.15, p=0.016), ruling out expectation drift; 125-session pseudo-participant hardware simulation yielded null results; post hoc lability correlation r=0.472, p=0.011 [catalog ID: spottiswoode_2003_skin]
- Alibalaei et al. (2025) -- Four pre-registered online forced-choice precognition experiments with yoga/meditation practitioners (total N=273) yielded non-significant primary results, but consistent post hoc psi-missing across all three experimental studies (e.g., 14 significant missers vs. 3 hitters, p<0.001); co-authored with Radin; note: this is a forced-choice precognition study, not a physiological presentiment study [catalog ID: alibalaei_2025_experimental]
- Roney-Dougal & Solfvin (2011) -- Free-response precognition with 10 Tibetan Buddhist monks (2 Lamas, 1 Rinpoche, 7 Geshes) across 8 sessions each: overall null (t(79)=0.70, p=0.49), but the two Lamas (most advanced, 3-year retreat graduates) showed significant psi-hitting (t(15)=2.25, p=0.04, r=0.50); combined with Study 1 (18 monks, 168 trials) monastic rank predicted psi (F(2,15)=4.33, p=0.033) and meditation-years correlation was rho=0.737, p=0.0005 [catalog ID: roney_dougal_2011_meditation_tibetan]
- Tressoldi (2011) -- Bayesian meta-analysis of anticipatory psycho-physiological responses (37 studies, 1,064 participants) yields BF=2.89×10^13, strongest evidence among all protocols examined [catalog ID: tressoldi_2011_extraordinary_claims]

**Con:**
- Schwarzkopf (2014) -- Methodological critique arguing presentiment effects are artifacts of baseline correction, stimulus timing, and data analysis choices [catalog ID: schwarzkopf_2014_should]
- Rabeyron (2020) -- Analysis of why most presentiment results cannot withstand methodological scrutiny, explicitly citing Ioannidis (2005) to argue that presentiment exhibits all six risk factors for false positives [catalog ID: rabeyron_2020_most]
- Ioannidis (2005) -- The foundational mathematical framework showing why small effects (d=0.21), small samples, and multiple paradigms tested make presentiment findings likely false positives; directly cited by Rabeyron (2020) [catalog ID: ioannidis_2005_false]
- Bierman (2020) -- Reports an absence of predictive anticipatory activity under tightly controlled conditions [catalog ID: bierman_2020_absence]
- Houran, Lange & Hooper (2018) -- Multidisciplinary commentary (with Fermi Lab physicist Dan Hooper) arguing precognition/presentiment effects are neither meaningful nor interpretable: small effect sizes do not exceed the "crap factor" in measurement, and retrocausation violates special/general relativity and the second law of thermodynamics; proposes transliminality and intuitive thinking as conventional explanations [catalog ID: houran_2018_crossexamining]

**Current status:** Presentiment has a stronger meta-analytic base than many other psi claims (d = 0.21 across 26 studies), and the effect has been reported across multiple physiological measures (EDA, EEG, heart rate, fMRI). However, critics argue the effect is an artifact of how baseline periods are defined, how stimuli are randomized, and how physiological signals are filtered. The 2014 Mossbridge et al. paper attempted to address these critiques directly, and the 2015 response to Schwarzkopf provided a detailed simulation showing expectation bias cannot account for most findings. Skeptics remain unconvinced. Notably, presentiment has attracted fewer high-profile direct rebuttals than Bem's work, possibly because the physiological methodology is more specialized. Recent pre-registered forced-choice precognition studies (Alibalaei et al. 2025) have reported null primary results but intriguing post hoc psi-missing, keeping the debate about unconscious anomalous cognition active.
