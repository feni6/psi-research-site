# 5. Does distant healing/prayer work?

The question of whether prayer, distant intention, or "healing energy" can influence health outcomes or biological systems at a distance spans two distinct paradigms: (1) clinical RCTs testing prayer or intercessory healing on patient health outcomes, and (2) laboratory studies testing whether DI from healers produces measurable physiological or neuroimaging responses in isolated recipients. The results diverge markedly between these two paradigms.

**Pro:**
- Byrd (1988) -- First large-scale double-blind RCT of intercessory prayer: 393 CCU patients randomized to prayer (n=192) or control (n=201); prayer group had significantly better severity scores (P<.01) and multivariate analysis separated groups at P<.0001 on antibiotics, diuretics, and intubation; no mortality difference [catalog ID: byrd_1988_prayer]
- Sicher et al. (1998) -- Double-blind RCT of distant healing in 40 advanced AIDS patients pair-matched on age, CD4+, and ADDs; treatment group had fewer new AIDS-defining illnesses (0.1 vs 0.6, P=0.04), lower illness severity (P=0.03), fewer doctor visits (P=0.01), fewer hospitalizations (P=0.04), and improved mood (P=0.02); multivariate randomization test across all 11 outcomes P=0.0154 [catalog ID: sicher_1998_distant_healing]
- Astin et al. (2000) -- Systematic review of distant healing efficacy finds positive trends across 23 trials [catalog ID: astin_2000_efficacy]
- Roe, Sonnex & Roxburgh (2015) -- Two meta-analyses updating Astin 2000 (excluding discredited Wirth studies): Phase 1 (49 non-whole-human studies) weighted r = .258, quality-filtered r = .115; Phase 2 (57 whole-human studies) weighted r = .203, quality-filtered r = .224; both significant, though publication bias and quality-outcome correlations noted [catalog ID: roe_2015_distant_healing]
- Harris et al. (1999) -- Double-blind RCT replicating Byrd (1988): 990 CCU patients randomized to intercessory prayer or usual care; prayer group had 11% lower weighted MAHI-CCU composite scores (P=.04), but result did not replicate using Byrd's original scoring system (P=.29) [catalog ID: harris_1999_prayer]
- Leibovici (2001) -- Double-blind RCT of retroactive intercessory prayer for 3,393 patients with bloodstream infection; prayer said 4-10 years after infections; hospital stay significantly shorter (median 7 vs 8 days, P=0.01) and fever duration shorter (P=0.04), though mortality difference non-significant (28.1% vs 30.2%, P=0.4); published in BMJ Christmas issue [catalog ID: leibovici_2001_retroactive_prayer]
- Krucoff et al. (2001) -- MANTRA I feasibility pilot: 150 PCI patients randomized to stress relaxation, imagery, touch therapy, off-site prayer, or standard care; 25-30% absolute reduction in adverse outcomes with noetic therapy (not significant); all 6-month mortality in noetic groups (9.2% vs 0%, P=.12) [catalog ID: krucoff_2001_integrative]
- Achterberg, Cooke, Richards, Standish, Kozak & Lake (2005) -- fMRI study with 11 healer-recipient pairs in Hawaii shows significant brain activation in recipients (anterior cingulate, precuneus, frontal regions; p=0.000127) correlated with healers' distant intentionality intervals; recipients were sensorially isolated from healers during the scan [catalog ID: achterberg_2005_evidence]
- Schlitz et al. (2012) -- NIH-funded 3-arm RCT (N=72) of distant healing intention on surgical wound healing; no significant group differences on primary outcome (collagen deposition, F(2,62)=0.79, P=.46) or secondary measures; post-hoc: breast cancer reconstruction patients in blinded DHI group showed significantly better mood (P=.004); healers' perceived connectedness negatively correlated with outcomes (mood rho=-0.57, P=.001; collagen rho=-0.30, P=.04) [catalog ID: schlitz_2012_distant]
- Schwartz & Dossey (2010) -- Narrative review using the STEP trial (N=1802) as a case study, arguing the pharmacological dose-dependent RCT model is fundamentally inappropriate for intention-healing research; integrates evidence from experimenter effects (Wiseman–Schlitz), Decision Augmentation Theory (May et al.), MANTRA II two-tier prayer, and Achterberg's healer fMRI study to propose that observer effects from all participants (including researchers and critics) must be evaluated in study design [catalog ID: schwartz_2010_nonlocality]
- Radin, Stone, Levine et al. (2008) -- 36 couples in DMILS design; sender directed compassionate intention while receiver sat in EM-shielded chamber; receiver skin conductance significantly elevated at intention-epoch offset (z = 3.9, p = .00009, 1,140 epochs); cancer-patient couples showed per-session effect size (e = 0.74) 6.7x larger than DMILS meta-analytic estimate; experimenter not fully blind to group assignment [catalog ID: radin_2008_compassionate]
- Braud & Schlitz (1991) -- Comprehensive review of 13-year DMILS program: 37 experiments across 8 living target systems (EDA, blood pressure, tremor, fish, gerbils, hemolysis) with 655 sessions; overall Stouffer z = 7.72, p = 2.58 × 10⁻¹⁴, mean r = .33; 57% of experiments independently significant; interpreted as laboratory analogs of mental healing [catalog ID: braud_schlitz_1991_consciousness_interactions]
- Braud (1993) -- Summarizes 15 DMILS electrodermal experiments at the Mind Science Foundation (323 sessions, 271 subjects, 62 influencers): 13/15 studies in expected direction, 6/15 (40%) independently significant vs. 5% chance baseline; combined Stouffer z = 4.08, p = .000023; mean effect size d = 0.29. Results obtained with unselected community volunteers using an automated skin resistance recording protocol. Establishes the canonical electrodermal DMILS paradigm adopted by later meta-analyses [catalog ID: braud_1993_remote_mental_influence_eda]
- Schmidt, Schneider, Utts & Walach (2004) -- Meta-analysis of 36 DMILS studies: quality-weighted d = 0.11 (p = .001, 95% CI [0.04, 0.17]), but best-evidence synthesis of 7 highest-quality studies non-significant (d = 0.05, p = .50); effect sizes negatively correlated with study quality [catalog ID: schmidt_2004_distant]
- Schmidt (2012) -- Meta-analysis of 11 AFFE studies (N = 576 sessions) yields d = 0.11 (p = 0.03, 95% CI [0.01, 0.22]); effect size converges with two earlier DMILS meta-analyses at d ~ 0.11–0.13 across 62 studies total [catalog ID: schmidt_2012_help]
- Bengston & Krinsley (2000) -- Four experiments at Queens College and St. Joseph's College in which Bengston and skeptical volunteer healers placed hands outside cages of mice with transplanted mammary adenocarcinoma (H2712 strain; 100% predicted fatality within 14–27 days) for 1 hour/day. Key results: 87.9% of 33 experimental mice achieved complete remission (vs. 0/8 off-site controls). Tumor regression followed a reproducible blackening → ulceration → implosion pattern; histology confirmed viable cancer cells throughout, consistent with an immune-mediated response; reinjected remitted mice were immune to the same cancer. Belief in healing was not required — all volunteer healers were self-described skeptics. Critical methodological flaw: on-site control mice that were "observed" by healer volunteers also remitted at 69.2% (18/26), undermining clean experimental control. Not independently replicated outside Bengston's group [catalog ID: bengston_2000_laying_on_hands]
- Beseme, Bengston, Radin, Turner & McMichael (2018) -- In vitro study: MDA-MB-231 breast carcinoma cells exposed to electromagnetic recording of 3 Bengston-method healers (R18) showed significant changes in 68/167 genes (p<0.05); ACLY consistently downregulated at 4hr across 3 independent experiments (fold: −1.35 to −2.11, p=.011 to .00007), IL-1β at 24hr (fold: −1.73/−1.61, p<.004); hands-on delivery faster and stronger (IL-1β fold=−4.07 at 1hr); no phenotypical cell changes observed [catalog ID: beseme_2018_transcriptional]
- Yu, Radin, Chu & Shiah (2024) -- Double-blind test: U87MG glioblastoma cells cultured in water treated by Buddhist monks' intention migrated 25% less than controls; time x water interaction F(3,9) = 8.560, p = 0.005 across 3 replications; extends the Shiah/Radin treated-water paradigm to a clinically relevant cancer cell line [catalog ID: yu_2024_effects]
- Schwartz, De Mattei, Brame & Spottiswoode (2015) -- Pilot study with 14 therapeutic practitioners: sealed sterile water vials held near palms during healing sessions showed significant IR spectral alteration in O-H bonding (ZnSe IRE: Treated vs calibration z=3.54, P=.0004; Treated vs all controls z=2.56, P=.005); Practicing practitioners P=.001 vs Non-practicing P=.04; temperature and barometric artifacts examined and ruled out; however, some session controls were also affected (P=.002 vs calibration), raising proximity contamination questions [catalog ID: schwartz_2015_infrared]
- Schwartz (2019) -- Twelve blind wine-tasting sessions (84 tasters total) found 11/12 majorities preferred intention-treated wine over control from same bottle (binomial p=0.00049); extends water spectroscopy and crystal formation findings to aesthetic judgment [catalog ID: schwartz_2019_water]
- Crawford, Jonas, Nelson, Wirkus & Wirkus (2003) -- REG in bioenergy healer's office showed excursions on 92% of days vs. 58% in parallel library control (χ²=16.3, p<0.0005); three independent datasets (1999-2001) consistent; effect independent of attention directed to REG [catalog ID: crawford_2003_alterations]
- Radin, Taft & Yount (2004) -- Four Johrei practitioners directed healing intention at astrocyte cultures in a shielded chamber over 3 days; no overall treatment effect (p=0.45) but significant treatment-by-day interaction (p=0.02) with day 3 treated cells growing more than controls; three continuously running RNGs peaked at combined z=4.8 (bootstrap p=0.00009) on day 3, supporting a cumulative space-conditioning hypothesis (combined z=4.15–4.32, p=0.00002–0.000008) [catalog ID: radin_2004_possible]
- Radin, Hayssen, Emoto & Kizu (2006) -- Pilot double-blind test of Emoto's water crystal hypothesis: ~2,000 Tokyo participants directed positive intentions toward water in a shielded room in California; 100 blind judges rated treated crystals significantly more beautiful than controls (mean 2.87 vs. 1.88, t(38) = 3.27, p < .001, d ≈ 1.04); replicated with a second set of 100 judges (p < .002) [catalog ID: radin_2006_doubleblind]
- Radin, Lund, Emoto & Kizu (2008) -- Triple-blind replication of water crystal intention study: ~1,900 distant intenders, 6 bottles (2 treated, 2 proximal control, 2 distant control), 300 crystal photos rated by 2,579 blind judges; treated crystals rated more beautiful (ANOVA p=0.03; treated vs proximal t(198)=1.67, p=0.05 one-tailed; beauty>1 subset p=0.01); combined with 2006 pilot Stouffer Z=3.34, p=0.0004; BUT distant controls slightly more beautiful than treated for all trials [catalog ID: radin_2008_effects]

**Con:**
- Benson et al. (2006) -- The STEP trial (Study of the Therapeutic Effects of Intercessory Prayer), the largest and most rigorous prayer study ever conducted (N=1,802), finds no benefit of intercessory prayer and a slight worsening in patients who knew they were being prayed for [catalog ID: benson_2006_therapeutic]
- Masters & Spielmans (2007) -- Updated meta-analysis of 15 distant intercessory prayer studies finds no discernible effect (g = 0.082, p = .26; g = 0.003, p = .97 without questionable Cha & Wirth study); no moderator variables significant [catalog ID: masters_2007_prayer]
- Krucoff et al. (2005) -- The MANTRA II trial (a follow-up to the 2001 study) finds no significant effect of noetic therapies including prayer on cardiac outcomes [catalog ID: krucoff_2005_music]
- Roberts, Ahmed & Davison (2009) -- Cochrane systematic review of 10 RCTs (N=7,646): no effect of intercessory prayer on death (RR 1.00, CI 0.74-1.36), clinical state (RR 0.98, CI 0.86-1.11), or rehospitalisation; 3 of 33 specific complications marginally favoured prayer but attributed to multiple testing; awareness of prayer associated with more complications (RR 1.15, CI 1.04-1.28) [catalog ID: roberts_2009_intercessory_prayer_cochrane]

**Current status:** The evidence picture is more nuanced than a simple pro/con split. The two largest individual clinical RCTs — the STEP prayer trial (Benson 2006, N=1,802) and MANTRA II (Krucoff 2005) — found no benefit of intercessory prayer, and the Cochrane systematic review (Roberts et al. 2009, 10 RCTs, N=7,646) found no clear effect on any primary outcome. However, the most comprehensive meta-analyses tell a different story: Roe et al. (2015) found significant positive effects across both non-whole-human biological systems (r = .115 quality-filtered, 22 studies) and whole-human clinical trials (r = .224 quality-filtered, 27 studies), even after excluding the discredited Wirth studies. These meta-analytic effect sizes are small but robust to quality filtering, though publication bias and quality-outcome correlations temper confidence. The laboratory DMILS literature adds further support: Schmidt's meta-analyses (2004, 2012) find small but significant effects, and Achterberg et al. (2005) found significant fMRI brain activation in recipients during DI intervals. Prayer studies specifically show the weakest effects (r = .138 cropped), while therapeutic touch and Reiki/Johrei show somewhat larger effects. The critical question is whether the positive meta-analytic and laboratory findings reflect genuine healing effects that large individual prayer RCTs are insensitive to, or whether the positive aggregate results reflect publication bias and the methodological flexibility of smaller studies.
