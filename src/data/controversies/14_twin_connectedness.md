# 14. Do identical twins show anomalous physiological connectedness?

A small but distinctive research program tests whether monozygotic twins who report exceptional telepathic experiences show anomalous physiological synchrony when one twin receives stimuli while the other is isolated. The paradigm uses polygraph monitoring (electrodermal, pulse, breathing, blood pressure) with a blinded expert attempting to identify stimulus windows from the remote twin's physiological record. Two pilot studies (Copenhagen and London) report that approximately 1 in 4 screened twin pairs show significant synchrony, though overall results are marginally significant.

**Pro:**
- Jensen & Parker (2012) -- "Entangled in the Womb?" pre-registered pilot study with 4 pairs of identical twins (3 usable); one twin received 5 randomized startle stimuli while the isolated twin was monitored via polygraph 100 m away. Overall results nonsignificant (P > .7), but the monochorionic-monoamnionic twin pair showed highly significant synchrony: first blind polygraph expert identified 3/5 stimulus times from 10 estimates (P = .03), hitting the three strongest stimuli; a second independent blind expert confirmed the same three hits from 8 estimates (P = .013). The largest response was a massive GSR rise in the unstimulated twin coinciding within 4 seconds of plates crashing behind the stimulated twin [catalog ID: jensen_2012_entangled]
- Parker & Jensen (2013) -- London replication attempt with four pairs of twins selected via Exceptional Experiences Questionnaire; forced-choice protocol with 24 assessed trials yielded 6 hits vs. 3 expected by chance (P = 0.07, one-tailed); one twin pair (youngest, age 25) produced 4 of 6 hits [catalog ID: parker_2013_further]
- Grinberg-Zylberbaum & Ramos (1987) -- Interhemispheric EEG correlation patterns become similar (r≈0.80) during direct communication without sensory contact in 13 pairs of subjects; intersubject EEG concordance increased from ~0 to 0.80 during pair communication; foundational for brain-brain interaction paradigm [catalog ID: grinberg_zylberbaum_1987_interhemispheric]
- Duane & Behrendt (1965) -- "Extrasensory Electroencephalographic Induction Between Identical Twins"; EEG alpha rhythm correlations between separated identical twins; one twin received stimuli while other showed correlated EEG changes; published in *Science* [catalog ID: duane_1965_extrasensory]
- Standish et al. (2003) -- First fMRI demonstration of correlated brain signals between distant subjects: receiver in Faraday-shielded 1.5T MRI scanner showed significant BOLD activation (P < .001) in visual cortex areas 18-19 correlated with sender's flickering checkerboard 10 m away; effect not observed when roles reversed; N=2 (one pair of colleagues, not twins); NIH-funded case report [catalog ID: standish_2003_evidence]
- Wackermann et al. (2003) -- Six-channel EEG recorded from 14 pairs (7 related, 7 unrelated) in shielded rooms; non-parametric randomization statistics showed outlier counts in non-stimulated subjects deviated strongly from Poisson fits vs. controls (G=14.13, P<0.01); notably, both related and unrelated pairs showed the effect equally, challenging the empathic-bond hypothesis [catalog ID: wackermann_2003_correlations]
- Tressoldi et al. (2016) -- EEG brain-to-brain interaction study extending the twin paradigm to non-twin adult meditator pairs (6 participants, 25 sessions); novel GW6 inter-electrode correlation algorithm detected a statistically significant Alpha-band response in isolated receivers during sender stimulation (p = 0.002, 9-10 Hz; Monte Carlo corrected); receiver latency ~700 ms vs. sender ~300 ms; effect magnitude ~0.5% correlation change; standard ERP averaging detected no signal in receivers [catalog ID: tressoldi_2016_brain_interaction]
- Richards et al. (2005) -- fMRI and EEG study at Bastyr/UW Consciousness Science Lab: one pre-selected pair (from 30 EEG pairs) showed significant BOLD activation in visual cortex (BA 17/18/19) correlated with isolated partner's flickering checkerboard in 3 of 4 fMRI trials (p < 0.017 Bonferroni corrected for ~30,000 voxels); EEG confirmed correlated alpha-power changes (CW: χ² = 455.4, p < .0001; DW: χ² = 317.4, p < .005); NIH/NCCAM funded (R21-AT00287); extends Standish et al. 2003/2004 from same lab [catalog ID: richards_2005_replicable]

**Con:**
- *[No direct skeptical replications or critiques of the twin physiological connectedness paradigm are currently in the library. The main limitations are the very small sample sizes (4 twin pairs per study), pilot-study status, and absence of independent replication by other laboratories.]*

**Current status:** This is the smallest evidence base in the library for any controversy: two pilot studies (Copenhagen and London) totaling 8 twin pairs and 14 sessions. Both studies found that approximately 1 in 4 screened pairs showed significant physiological synchrony, but overall results were marginally significant at best. The strongest finding came from the Copenhagen study's monochorionic-monoamnionic twin pair (sharing same placenta and amnion cavity), which has led to speculation about embryonic entanglement. The London study had methodological limitations compared to Copenhagen (iPhone SMS timing vs. atomic clocks, no inter-rater reliability check). The key missing elements are: (1) independent replication by a laboratory other than Parker/Jensen, (2) larger sample sizes with pre-registered analysis plans, and (3) exploration of whether the monochorionic-monoamnionic subgroup shows systematically stronger effects. The Duane (1965) EEG study in *Science* provides historical precedent but used different methodology. This controversy remains in the exploratory pilot-study phase.
