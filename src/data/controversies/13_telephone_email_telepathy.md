# 13. Does the telephone/email telepathy paradigm demonstrate anomalous information transfer?

Rupert Sheldrake and Pamela Smart developed a simple, repeatable protocol to test whether people can identify callers before answering a phone: one of four known callers is chosen at random (by die throw), and the participant names the caller when the phone rings, without caller ID. Across more than 850 trials with 65 participants (2003), the average hit rate was 42% vs. 25% chance (p = 1×10⁻²⁶). Subsequent experiments extended the paradigm to email and web-based automated systems. Skeptics argue that the effects reflect subtle methodological flaws (timing cues, insufficient randomization controls, lack of independent replication) rather than genuine anomalous communication.

**Pro:**
- Sheldrake (2000) -- Two random telephone surveys in UK (London N=387, Bury N=200) establishing baseline prevalence of telepathic telephone experiences in general population: 51% felt someone was going to telephone before they did (London); 65% had called someone who said they were just thinking of calling them (Bury); 49% knew who was calling without cues; women significantly more likely than men (56% vs 41%, p < 0.0002); notably more people anticipated calls than reported psychic experiences (51% vs 38%, p < 0.0004), suggesting telephone anticipation may be entry-point psi phenomenon [catalog ID: sheldrake_2000_telephone_surveys]
- Brown & Sheldrake (2001) -- Telephone survey of 200 randomly selected California residents; 78% reported calling someone who said they were just thinking of calling them, 47% knew who was calling without cues, 68% thought of someone who then called same day; foundational survey establishing high prevalence of telephone telepathy experiences; women reported higher rates but differences not significant [catalog ID: brown_sheldrake_2001_anticipation]
- Sheldrake (2002) -- Survey of 100 nursing mothers; 16% reported milk let-down coinciding with baby's needs when separated; foundational evidence for spontaneous telepathy in close relationships; chi-squared=8.67, P<0.005 for breastfeeding duration correlation [catalog ID: sheldrake_2002_telepathy_babies]
- Sheldrake & Smart (2003) -- Videotaped telephone telepathy experiments with 4 participants across 271 valid trials using four progressively rigorous methods (final method: all callers filmed at separate location by independent cameramen); overall 45% hit rate vs. 25% chance (p = 10⁻¹², 95% CI [39%, 51%]); familiar callers 61% (p = 10⁻¹³), unfamiliar 20% (ns); confidence ratings predicted accuracy (82% when confident, p = 3.6 × 10⁻¹⁰); increased rigor did not reduce hit rates [catalog ID: sheldrake_2003_videotaped]
- Sheldrake, Godwin & Rockell (2004) -- Filmed TV replication with the Nolan Sisters; 12 trials, Colleen Nolan 1 km from callers, blind video evaluation; 50% hit rate, p=0.05 [catalog ID: sheldrake_2004_nolan_telephone]
- Sheldrake & Smart (2005) -- Email telepathy extension (4-sender protocol, 702 trials across two series); Series 1 unfilmed: 43% vs. 25% chance (p=2×10⁻¹⁹, d=0.42); Series 2 with continuous video surveillance: 47% (p=3×10⁻⁸, d=0.50); time-stamped server-side e-mail records prevent retroactive tampering [catalog ID: sheldrake_2005_email_telepathy]
- Sheldrake & Lambert (2007) -- Automated online telepathy test with 195 receivers across 1,980 trials (4-sender protocol with real and virtual senders); overall 29.3% vs 25% chance (p = 0.000006, 95% CI 27-31%); family senders yielded higher hit rates than non-family (31.4% vs 27.5%, p = 0.02); unsupervised conditions could not rule out cheating [catalog ID: sheldrake_lambert_2007_online_telepathy]
- Sheldrake & Beharee (2009) -- Rapid online telepathy test with 500 participants completing 6,000 trials; 30-sec trials, two actual and two virtual senders; overall 26.7% vs. 25% chance (p=0.002, d=0.03); videotaped subset (468 trials) showed 27.3% with no cheating detected; hit rate declined with repeated testing (45.2% → 35.2% → 24.4%); demonstrates above-chance results in large-scale automated unsupervised setting but much smaller effect size than telephone/email studies [catalog ID: sheldrake_2009_online_telepathy]
- Sheldrake (2014) -- Comprehensive overview synthesizing 15+ years of telephone/email/SMS telepathy research; telephone tests 40-45% vs 25% chance, email 43-47%, SMS 37.9-44.2%, automated phone 56% vs 50%; confidence strongly predicted accuracy (85% when 'confident'); precognition control tests at chance level supporting telepathy mechanism [catalog ID: sheldrake_2014_telepathy_phone_text]
- Sheldrake & Avraamides (2009) -- Fully automated, experimenter-free internet-based telepathy test (3-sender protocol); 419 trials; 41.8% vs. 33.3% chance (p=0.0001, d=0.20); effect size substantially smaller than supervised simultaneous tests (d=0.5), which authors attribute to asynchronous timing eliminating sender focus at the moment of guessing [catalog ID: sheldrake_2009_automated_email]
- Sheldrake, Avraamides & Novák (2009) -- SMS telepathy extension with fully automated system; 886 trials, participants aged 11-72; 37.9% vs. 33.3% chance (p=.001, d=0.10); incomplete tests 38.4% (p=.03) ruling out optional stopping; 5 high-scorers retested filmed: 44.2% (19/43, p=.09); effect size smaller than telephone/email studies due to unsupervised design and asynchronous timing [catalog ID: sheldrake_2009_sms_telepathy]
- Sheldrake, Smart & Avraamides (2015) -- First automated mobile phone telepathy experiments; 2080 trials with three callers (41.8% vs 33.3%, p<10^-15, d=0.19) and 745 trials with two callers (55.2% vs 50%, p=.003, d=0.10); incomplete tests 43.8% (p=.00003) ruling out optional stopping; online pre-registration, fully automated; effect sizes smaller than supervised studies, possibly due to convenience sampling and lack of screening for intuitive participants [catalog ID: sheldrake_smart_avraamides_2015_automated_telephone]
- Sheldrake (2019) -- Theoretical article proposing morphic fields as explanatory framework for telepathy; synthesizes evidence from telephone/email telepathy experiments and other paradigms; positions telepathy as biological phenomenon occurring between bonded social group members [catalog ID: sheldrake_2019_morphic_fields_telepathy]
- Sheldrake & Smart (2003a) -- Foundational 4-caller home-based protocol study; 63 participants and 571 trials yielded 40% hits vs. 25% chance (Stouffer combined p = 4x10⁻⁶); familiar callers 53% vs unfamiliar 25% (p = 3x10⁻⁷); established the core design later used in videotaped and independent replications [catalog ID: sheldrake_smart_2003_experimental_tests_telephone]
- Liu (2023) -- Student meta-analysis of 16 telepathy experiments (21,441 trials, 9 articles) from 2002-2015; overall weighted effect size 0.091; 75% of experiments showed significant above-chance hit rates; methodologically limited (no heterogeneity or publication bias analysis) but consistent with a small positive effect [catalog ID: liu_2023_detecting_telepathy_meta]
- Lobach & Bierman (2004) -- Prospective replication testing Spottiswoode's (1997) LST hypothesis: N=6 participants, 214 trials; overall 29.4% hit rate (p=0.05, one-tailed); peak-LST sessions 34.6% vs. non-peak 25.2% (p=0.09 trend); emotional bond correlated with hit rate (r=0.41, p<0.05); first prospective test of the LST modulation hypothesis in the telephone telepathy paradigm, though local time and LST are confounded due to Amsterdam winter scheduling [catalog ID: lobach_bierman_2004_lst_telephone]
- Wahbeh et al. (2024) -- Pre-registered automated telephone telepathy study (N=177, triads); telepathic/pre-selected trials 48.1% vs 33.3% chance (p < .001), precognitive/post-selected trials 32.5% (ns); communication frequency predicted accuracy (P = .03); favors telepathic over precognitive mechanism [catalog ID: wahbeh_2024_who_calling_accuracy]
- Sheldrake & Stedall (2024) -- Four automated telephone telepathy experiments comparing conference-call and separated-trial formats; Exps 1–3 (conference call) showed no significant effects (51–52%, 1,725 total trials), but Exp 4 (separated trials, 266 trials) yielded 57% hit rate (p = .01); positive effect widely distributed (36 positive vs 17 negative tests, p = .006); pre-registered on OSF [catalog ID: sheldrake_stedall_2024_automated_telephone]
- Sheldrake, Stedall & Tressoldi (2025) -- Meta-analysis of 26 experiments from 15 papers across telephone, email, SMS, and web-based paradigms; telepathy-condition effect 8.7% above chance (95% CI 5.3-11.9, p=4x10⁻8), while precognition-condition tests were near chance [catalog ID: sheldrake_2025_telecommunication_telepathy_meta]

**Con:**
- Schmidt, Erath, Ivanova & Walach (2009) -- Independent replication attempt at University of Freiburg with pre-deposited protocols and videotaped trials: Study 1 (N=21, office) 26.7% hit rate (ns, h=0.04); Study 2 (N=8, home) 30% (p=.15, h=0.11); overall 557 trials yielded p=.02 driven entirely by one exceptional participant (42.5% over 80 trials, p=.00015); no group-level anomalous cognition detected [catalog ID: schmidt_2009_who_is_calling]

**See also:** [Who's Calling?](/stories#telephone-telepathy) (Remarkable Experiments story) | [Replication chain](/replications#8-telephone-telepathy)

**Current status:** The telephone telepathy evidence base now includes a full 2025 quantitative synthesis: across 23 telepathy-condition experiments, performance was 8.7% above chance (standardized ES = 0.17), with moderator patterns favoring selected participants and emotionally bonded pairs; three precognition-condition datasets were near chance. This strengthens the aggregate pro-psi signal while preserving key skeptical concerns: substantial heterogeneity, a relatively small independent-replication subset, and inconsistent group-level performance in Freiburg (Schmidt et al., 2009). The main unresolved issue is whether preregistered multi-lab studies can reliably identify high-performing participants without post hoc selection. The foundational population surveys (Sheldrake 2000; Brown & Sheldrake 2001) establish that spontaneous telephone telepathy experiences are common in the general population (45-65% depending on question), providing ecological context for the laboratory experiments.
