# 10. Is psi research fundamentally flawed? (The meta-debate)

Beyond any individual controversy, there is a meta-level debate about whether the entire field of psi research is capable of producing reliable knowledge. Skeptics argue that persistent methodological problems, publication bias, the decline effect, and the absence of a physical mechanism make psi an unfalsifiable research program. Proponents counter that psi research has actually driven methodological innovation (pre-registration, Bayesian methods, meta-analysis) and that the evidence, while small, is consistent across paradigms.

**Pro (psi research is sound and produces meaningful evidence):**
- Rhine (1934) -- *Extra-Sensory Perception* established the experimental paradigm for psi research with 64,000+ card-guessing trials at Duke University. Introduced Zener card methodology, statistical analysis using probable error and critical ratios (X values up to 75.3), and documented telepathy, clairvoyance, decline effects, and displacement effects. Founded the Journal of Parapsychology (1937) and launched the research program that continues today [catalog ID: rhine_1934_extrasensory_perception]
- Cardena (2018) -- "The Experimental Evidence for Parapsychological Phenomena" in American Psychologist argues the cumulative evidence meets the standards accepted in other domains of psychology [catalog ID: cardena_2018_experimental]
- Schwartz (2019) -- Applied RV field experiment at Marea, Egypt: two viewers under triple-blind conditions correctly located and described a 6th-century Byzantine structure at a site where a 1976 magnetometer survey had found nothing; the paper presents a direct head-to-head comparison where conventional electronic sensing was completely unproductive and RV produced the only positive predictive data [catalog ID: schwartz_2019_location]
- Cardeña et al. (2014) -- Collective opinion statement in *Frontiers in Human Neuroscience* signed by ~100 academics (including Nobel laureate Josephson, Utts, Bem, Zimbardo) arguing that psi research is conducted at accredited universities, has survived increasing methodological controls, and should not be dismissed a priori [catalog ID: cardena_2014_call_open_consciousness]
- Tressoldi (2011) -- "Extraordinary Claims Require Extraordinary Evidence" argues that psi results actually do meet this standard when assessed by the same criteria applied elsewhere; Bayesian analysis shows BF=18.8M (ganzfeld), BF=25.4B (remote viewing), BF=2.89×10^13 (anticipatory responses) [catalog ID: tressoldi_2011_extraordinary; companion: tressoldi_2011_extraordinary_claims]
- Tressoldi & Storm (2021) -- Umbrella review of 11 meta-analyses (928 studies, 1989-2021) finds all 16 effect sizes significant except one; altered states of consciousness and free-response protocols are strong moderators (rs = .81, p = 1.9 × 10⁻⁴); 54-81% of individual studies non-significant, arguing against QRPs [catalog ID: tressoldi_2021_umbrella_review]
- Tressoldi (2012) -- Retrospective power analysis of four controversial phenomena (subliminal priming, incubation, unconscious thought, non-local perception) argues that replication failures reflect inadequate sample sizes, not absent phenomena; forced-choice ESP requires N=3,450 for 0.90 power but typical studies average N=128 [catalog ID: tressoldi_2012_replication]
- Schooler, Baumgart & Franklin (2018) -- "Entertaining Without Endorsing" argues that scientists' differing Bayesian priors rationally produce divergent evidence appraisals; reviews meta-analyses across four psi domains (precognition z=6.02, ganzfeld z=5.48, PK z=15.76) and proposes nine criteria for endorsing anomalous cognition; the "entertain without endorse" framework provides an epistemological middle ground from a prominent mainstream cognitive psychologist [catalog ID: schooler_2018_entertaining]
- Heino, Fried & LeBel (2017) -- Commentary arguing that complexity of psychological phenomena should motivate sophisticated designs (within-person time series, better operationalizations) rather than abandoning replicability standards; counters Iso-Ahola's claim that reproducibility is 'unattainable'; relevant because the 'phenomena are too complex to replicate' argument is used identically in parapsychology [catalog ID: heino_2017_reproducibility]
- Tressoldi (2018) -- "Advancing Psi Research" outlines standards for next-generation experiments
- Vieten et al. (2018) -- Largest survey of extraordinary experiences in meditators (N=1,120, 18 co-authors from IONS task force): 56% report clairvoyance/telepathy, 31% report PK-like external physical phenomena, 82% synchronicities; clairvoyance most strongly correlated with meditation practice length (r=.30); 25% psychiatric history comparable to general population (argues against pathological explanation); provides population-level justification for using meditators as psi research participants [catalog ID: vieten_2018_future]
- Lindahl et al. (2017) -- Mixed-methods study (N=60) documenting full range of meditation-related experiences across 7 domains (59 categories); 73% reported moderate-severe impairment, but valence ranged from very positive to very negative; provides empirical taxonomy distinguishing meditation-induced anomalous experiences from psychopathology; challenges assumption that only vulnerable populations experience difficulties [catalog ID: lindahl_2017_varieties_contemplative]
- Wahbeh et al. (2018) -- Anonymous survey of 899 US adults (general population, scientists/engineers, IONS enthusiasts) finds no significant difference in EHE prevalence between scientists (93.2%) and the general public (94.0%); paranormal belief explains 37% of EHE variance; dissociation and psychotic symptoms did not reach pathological levels in any group; challenges the assumption that scientific training suppresses anomalous experience [catalog ID: wahbeh_2018_exceptional]
- Wahbeh et al. (2019) -- Validation of the 20-item Noetic Experience and Belief Scale (NEBS), which separately measures paranormal belief (α=0.90) and experience (α=0.93) as distinct latent constructs; tested in 361 general-population and 646 IONS participants; provides standardized measurement tools enabling more rigorous experimental designs [catalog ID: wahbeh_2019_noetic]
- Wahbeh, Radin, Cannard & Delorme (2022) -- "What if consciousness is not an emergent property of the brain?" Frontiers in Psychology review examining six phenomena challenging materialist models: remote viewing meta-analyses, ganzfeld telepathy (31% hit rate), presentiment (Mossbridge 2012: d=0.21, p<2.71×10⁻¹²), xenoglossy/acquired savants, terminal lucidity (80%+ remission in dementia), reverse priming (z=6.4, p=1.2×10⁻¹⁰); compares physicalist theories (GWT, HOT, IIT) to non-local models (Orch-OR, analytic idealism, interface theory); proposes consciousness may be fundamental rather than emergent [catalog ID: wahbeh_2022_consciousness_not_emergent]
- Walach, von Lucadou & Römer (2014) -- Proposes Generalized Quantum Theory (GQT) as a theoretical framework for all psi phenomena; derives the No-Transmission (NT) axiom predicting that classical experimental designs will fail in exact replications because they code nonlocal correlations as signals; explains the decline effect in Bösch et al.'s PK meta-analysis and why meta-analyses remain positive while decisive replications fail [catalog ID: walach_2014_entanglement]
- Walach (2020) -- "Inner Experience – Direct Access to Reality" argues for complementarist dual-aspect monism where consciousness and matter are coprimary; critiques materialist ontology as unable to explain consciousness itself or anomalous cognition; proposes contemplative science as complementary epistemology; synthesizes philosophy of mind, consciousness studies, and parapsychology meta-analyses [catalog ID: walach_2020_inner_experience]
- Bem & Honorton (1994) -- Published the autoganzfeld results in *Psychological Bulletin*, a premier APA journal; 32% hit rate (z=2.89, p=.002) across 329 sessions with automated controls, demonstrating that psi research can meet mainstream methodological standards [catalog ID: bem_1994_does]
- Rabeyron (2022) -- "When the Truth Is Out There: Counseling People Who Report Anomalous Experiences" presents a clinical framework (PPAE) for working with anomalous experience reporters without pathologization or uncritical acceptance; describes three-step therapeutic model based on 750+ counseling sessions at CIRCEE since 2009; demonstrates that clinical parapsychology has developed sophisticated, ethically-grounded practice standards [catalog ID: kramer_2022_counseling_anomalous]
- Rao & Palmer (1987) -- BBS target article with 35 peer commentaries reviewing 100+ years of psi evidence; argues Schmidt's REG results (p < 10⁻¹⁰), ganzfeld replication rate (45%, cumulative Z = 6.60), and the noise reduction model collectively establish a strong prima facie case for statistical replicability; Alcock's companion critique and extensive peer commentary make this a self-contained map of the 1980s debate [catalog ID: rao_1987_anomaly]
- Utts (1991) -- Published in *Statistical Science* with invited commentary from six prominent statisticians; synthesizes four independent meta-analyses (ganzfeld, precognition, RNG-PK, dice-PK) all showing significant effects; autoganzfeld h=0.20 is triple the aspirin-heart attack effect; Bayarri & Berger Bayes factor 100-200:1 favoring psi; Hyman-Honorton Joint Communique agreed effects cannot be explained by selective reporting [catalog ID: utts_1991_replication_meta_analysis]
- Utts (1996) -- CIA-commissioned systematic review of SRI (770 sessions, ES=0.209) and SAIC (445 sessions, ES=0.230) remote viewing programs; expert viewers replicated ES≈0.35 across institutions and years; SAIC Experiment 10 yielded ES=0.55 (z=5.22, p=9.1×10⁻⁸); independent ganzfeld replications at four laboratories showed consistent 32-37% hit rates against 25% chance; concludes anomalous cognition is well established by standard scientific criteria [catalog ID: utts_1996_assessment]
- Bem, Utts & Johnson (2011) -- Reply to Wagenmakers et al. demonstrating that Bayesian conclusions are highly sensitive to prior specification: the same nine precognition experiments yield combined BF = 13,669 (knowledge-based prior) vs BF = 0.63 (Cauchy prior); argues Bayesian methods contain "hidden traps" when priors are poorly specified [catalog ID: bem_utts_johnson_2011_must_psychologists]
- Josephson & Pallikari-Viras (1991) -- "Biological Utilisation of Quantum Nonlocality" proposes a physics-compatible framework for psi [catalog ID: josephson_1991_biological]
- Kauffman & Radin (2023) -- Proposes a non-substance dualism (Res potentia / Res extensa linked by mind-mediated measurement) based on Heisenberg's concept of quantum "potentia"; reviews cumulative evidence across ganzfeld (30.6% hit rate, BF = 18.8 million), forced-choice precognition (11σ), presentiment (5.3–6.9σ), RNG-PK (6.8σ), and double-slit experiments (cumulative p < 4 × 10⁻¹¹); published in *BioSystems*, a mainstream biology journal; co-authored by emeritus UPenn theoretical biologist (Kauffman) and IONS experimenter (Radin) [catalog ID: kauffman_2023_quantum]
- Honorton & Ferrari (1989) -- Meta-analysis of 309 forced-choice precognition studies (62 investigators, 50+ years) finds no relationship between methodological quality and effect size (r = .081, p = .202); quality-weighted z is slightly larger than unweighted; high-quality studies (ratings 6-8) yield larger effects than low-quality studies (ratings 0-1) — directly contradicting the claim that psi effects are driven by poor methodology [catalog ID: honorton_1989_future]
- Jahn & Dunne (2005) -- "The PEAR Proposition" reviews 26 years of REG experiments (91 operators, ~2.5 million trials, composite p ~ 7×10⁻⁵) and 653 remote perception trials (Z > 5.4, p ~ 3×10⁻⁸); argues the PEAR program demonstrates that consciousness-related anomalies are legitimate, amenable to scientific study, and robust over enormous datasets despite irregular replicability [catalog ID: jahn_2005_pear]
- Escola-Gascon et al. (2023) -- Follow-up on CIA RV experiments with 634 participants, triple-blind forced-choice design with SEM invariance analysis addressing Hyman (1996) and Utts (1996) methodological criticisms; believers with image targets scored d=0.853 above chance (BF10=60.5); total sample d=0.457 matching average SAIC effect; total EI (experiential + strategic) predicted 19.5% of RV hit variance; demonstrates modern psi research can employ Bayesian inference, SEM, and triple-blind controls [catalog ID: escola_gascon_2023_cia_remote_viewing]
- Pehlivanova, Weiler & Greyson (2024) -- Cross-sectional survey (N=144) comparing actively open-minded thinking (AOT) and need for closure (NFC) among academic psi researchers, lay believers, academic skeptics, and lay skeptics. Psi researchers scored identically to academic skeptics on AOT (4.5±0.3 vs 4.5±0.3, p=0.91) and showed no difference in NFC (p=0.67); only lay believers scored lower on AOT. Challenges the 'cognitive deficit hypothesis' by demonstrating that psi researchers' high belief reflects evidence evaluation, not deficient reasoning [catalog ID: pehlivanova_2024_cognitive_styles]
- Baptista, Derakhshani & Tressoldi (2014) -- Comprehensive cross-paradigm review of ESP evidence (ganzfeld, forced-choice, RV, dream ESP) showing consistent above-chance effects across all four paradigms spanning 1935-2014; identifies participant selection as a robust moderator variable (e.g., selected ganzfeld HR = 40.1%, optimal forced-choice studies 87.5% significant); recommends pre-registration, power analysis, and modern meta-analytic methods [catalog ID: baptista_2015_explicit_anomalous_cognition]
- Sheldrake (2021) -- "Is the Sun Conscious?" argues for panpsychist and electromagnetic field theories of consciousness applied to astronomical scales; challenges mechanistic materialism by proposing consciousness may exist in self-organizing systems at all levels including stars and galaxies; provides theoretical framework compatible with psi phenomena [catalog ID: sheldrake_2021_sun_conscious]
- Kennedy (2001) -- Reviews 11 hypotheses for why psi is elusive and proposes integrative model: bimodal distribution of psi experiences, psi practitioners as ~1% of population, and goal-oriented experimenter effects; concludes cumulative meta-analytic evidence "favors the hypothesis that the elusive nature of psi is largely caused by factors other than methodological flaws" [catalog ID: kennedy_2001_psi_elusive]
- Kennedy (2003) -- Documents that psi effects are "unsustainable": negative reliability (direction reversals), meta-analysis demolition (90% effect-size reduction after summary), pervasive decline effects across subjects/experimenters/research lines, and failed practical applications (Star Gate, silver futures, land mines); proposes three hypotheses including competing background psi motivation and a higher consciousness inducing wonder — arguing these patterns reflect a genuine property of psi rather than methodological failure [catalog ID: kennedy_2003_capricious_psi]
- Radin, Nelson, Dobyns & Houtkooper (2006) -- Defense of RNG meta-analytic evidence against four concurrent critiques (Schub, Scargle, Ehm, Bösch et al.); demonstrates that standard funnel-plot publication bias detection fails when applied to inherently heterogeneous data (simulated constant-z data produce identical asymmetry without selective reporting); introduces the Experimenters' Regress (Collins, 1992) into the psi debate — demands for 'perfect' experiments or 'certain' results are epistemologically incoherent for pre-theoretical phenomena; a direct researcher survey found ~1 unreported study per investigator [catalog ID: radin_2006_assessing]
- Kennedy (2011) -- "Information in Life, Consciousness, Quantum Physics, and Paranormal Phenomena" proposes supernatural agency model: paranormal phenomena result from external agencies with spiritual motivations rather than being human abilities; explains psi's capricious, evasive nature and why it defies experimental control; integrates information theory, quantum physics, and parapsychology [catalog ID: kennedy_2011_information_consciousness]
- Kennedy (2006) -- Book review of "Irreducible Mind" argues for moving beyond narrow laboratory controversies toward a broader Myers-inspired theoretical framework; discusses psi's capricious, evasive nature as evidence for spiritual dimensions of consciousness rather than methodological nuisance [catalog ID: kennedy_2006_spirituality]
- Child (1985) -- Published in *American Psychologist*, documents how five psychology textbooks either ignored, distorted, or falsified the Maimonides dream ESP experiments; includes independent reanalysis showing combined p < .000002 for judges; a case study in systematic misrepresentation of parapsychological evidence by mainstream psychology [catalog ID: child_1985_psychology_anomalous]
- Kennedy (1981) -- Examines four cases of skeptical error: Gardner's misrepresentation of Coover's ESP results, Wheeler's fabricated fraud accusation against Rhine at the 1979 AAAS, Wilson's strategically underpowered ESP replication (reducing 6,210 to 450 trials after p<.005), and Zelen et al.'s post-hoc subgroup analysis of the significant Mars effect; argues negative results by skeptics require the same methodological scrutiny as positive findings [catalog ID: kennedy_1981_skepticism_negative_results]
- Kugel (2011) -- Demonstrates that the BSB (2006) PK meta-analysis in *Psychological Bulletin* — the most widely cited skeptical treatment of the PK evidence base — contains serious errors: inclusion of ~40 ESP studies in a PK database, fabricated z-scores, unrelated control data, and an overall negative result driven entirely by three potentially malfunctioning PEAR high-speed RNG studies; without those studies z = +3.59 [catalog ID: kugel_2011_faulty_pk_meta]
- Gigerenzer (2004) -- "Mindless Statistics" demonstrates that the 'null ritual' (NHST as practiced in psychology) is an incoherent hybrid of Fisher and Neyman-Pearson theories that neither statistician endorsed; survey evidence shows ~90% of psychology students, professors, and statistics teachers hold false beliefs about p-values; Meehl's conjecture that ~50% of directional predictions are confirmed by chance in large non-experimental samples is empirically supported with N>81,000 MMPI-2 subjects (Waller 2004: 46%); average statistical power in psychology ~50% for medium effects (Cohen 1962) and had declined by 1989. Directly applicable to psi meta-debate: null results are uninformative without prior power specification, and p-value-based dismissals of psi are undermined by the same ritual's pervasive misapplication across mainstream psychology [catalog ID: gigerenzer_2004_mindless_statistics]

**Con (psi research is fundamentally compromised):**
- Diaconis (1978) -- "Statistical Problems in ESP Research" in *Science* (Vol. 201): the foundational statistical critique of Rhine-era ESP research by a Stanford statistician and former professional magician. Identifies optional stopping, detection of pseudo-random target regularities by subjects, multiple testing with selective reporting, and inadequate sensory isolation as the principal sources of false positives. Demonstrates that methodological quality and reported effect size are inversely correlated across the historical ESP literature. Directly motivated methodological reforms (pre-registration, automated randomization, double-blind protocols) that shaped the post-1986 research agenda [catalog ID: diaconis_1978_statistical_esp]
- Marks (1982) -- "Remote Viewing Revisited" in *The Skeptical Inquirer*: documents Targ and Puthoff's multi-year refusal to release raw SRI transcripts, then demonstrates through 'remote judging' that New Zealand judges (12,000 km from the target sites) achieve p=.001 matching Hammid transcripts against targets using only 24 identifiable sensory cues—without any psi—completely accounting for the claimed RV effect; a systematic review of replication attempts finds all well-controlled RV studies yield null results while positive results appear exclusively in flawed designs, establishing the definitive methodological critique of the SRI program [catalog ID: marks_1982_remote_viewing_revisited]
- LeBel & Peters (2011) -- Argues that three systemic deficiencies in modal research practice (MRP) — overemphasis on conceptual replication, absent measurement integrity verification, and flawed NHST (nil hypothesis testing) — create an "interpretation bias" that buffers any theory from falsification, including parapsychological ones; uses Bem (2011) as a case study but explicitly argues these are general problems in empirical psychology; conservatism in theory choice strongly favors a methodological over a parapsychological interpretation of any psi evidence produced within MRP [catalog ID: lebel_peters_2011_fearing_future]
- Reber & Alcock (2019) -- "Searching for the Impossible" argues that psi violates well-established physical laws and that no amount of statistical evidence can overcome this prior improbability [catalog ID: reber_2019_searching]
- Alcock (2003) -- "Give the Null Hypothesis a Chance" contends that psi research has never produced a single reliable, independently replicable demonstration [catalog ID: alcock_2003_give]
- Alcock (2011) -- "Back from the Future" places Bem's claims in a historical pattern of claimed psi breakthroughs (Rhine, Schmidt, Targ/Puthoff, PEAR, ganzfeld) that were each followed by methodological unraveling; argues this recurring cycle demonstrates a fundamental flaw in parapsychological methodology [catalog ID: alcock_2011_back_from_future]
- Hyman (1994) -- Demonstrates that the autoganzfeld positive effect is confounded with target occurrence frequency and experimenter prompting (chi-squared = 14.702, p = .0001), illustrating how secondary re-analysis can reveal artifacts invisible in original reports [catalog ID: hyman_1994_anomaly]
- Hyman (1996) -- Commissioned Stargate evaluation argues parapsychology uniquely lacks a cumulative database (each generation discards predecessors' evidence), has no exemplar experiment a student could replicate with confidence, and defines its phenomenon negatively (any departure from chance = psi); concedes SAIC effect sizes are too large to be chance but contends statistical significance alone cannot establish anomalous cognition without a positive theory specifying boundary conditions [catalog ID: hyman_1996_evaluation]
- Hyman (2010) -- Commentary on Storm et al. arguing that meta-analysis provides a "buffer" between investigators and original data, masking fundamental inconsistencies: four experimenters drove the entire original ganzfeld effect (44% vs. 26% for others); autoganzfeld significance derived solely from dynamic targets not in the original paradigm; Autoganzfeld II (meeting all Storm et al.'s criteria, 94% power) produced chance results; compares psi meta-analyses to hypothetical N-ray meta-analysis (180 successful experiments) to argue significant composite effect sizes do not establish replicability [catalog ID: hyman_2010_meta_analysis_conceals]
- Jefferys (1990) -- "Bayesian Analysis of Random Event Generator Data" applies Bayesian hypothesis testing to the PEAR REG dataset, demonstrating via the Jeffreys-Lindley paradox that the classical p < 0.0003 overestimates evidence by a factor of >= 20; under most reasonable priors the Bayes factor favors the null; an early and influential argument for replacing frequentist p-values with Bayesian methods in parapsychology [catalog ID: jefferys_1990_bayesian_rng]
- Wagenmakers et al. (2011) -- "Why Psychologists Must Change the Way They Analyze Their Data": Bayesian reanalysis of Bem's nine precognition experiments finds evidence is 'anecdotal' at best (BF01 range 0.17–7.61); argues that standard p-value methodology is too weak and malleable for controversial claims, proposing pre-registration, Bayesian hypothesis testing, and adversarial collaboration as remedies; catalyzed the replication crisis conversation in mainstream psychology [catalog ID: wagenmakers_2011_why_psychologists]
- Rouder, Morey & Province (2013) -- Bayesian reanalysis of Storm et al.'s (2010) 67-study free-response meta-analysis: full dataset yields BF₁₀ ≈ 6 billion, but manually randomized studies show significantly inflated hit rates (BF ≈ 6,350 for difference); revised datasets excluding manual randomization and including omitted null conditions yield BF₁₀ ≈ 32–328; demonstrates how randomization quality and study selection can attenuate meta-analytic evidence by eight orders of magnitude [catalog ID: rouder_2013_bayes_factor_esp]
- Galak et al. (2012) -- Failed replications and a corrective meta-analysis demonstrating that the precognition evidence base is inflated by publication bias [catalog ID: galak_2012_correcting]
- Francis (2012) -- Applies the Ioannidis & Trikalinos test for excess significance to Bem's 10 psi experiments: pooled g* = 0.186 predicts 6.27 rejections out of 10 but 9 were observed (p = .058), indicating publication bias renders the set scientifically uninformative [catalog ID: francis_2012_publication_bias]
- Ioannidis (2005) -- "Why Most Published Research Findings Are False" provides mathematical proof that most research claims are false due to low power, bias, and multiple testing. Using the PPV (Positive Predictive Value) formula PPV = (1-β)R/(R - βR + α), Ioannidis demonstrates that for most research designs, PPV < 50%. The paper identifies six 'corollaries' making findings more likely false: (1) small studies, (2) small effects, (3) multiple tested relationships without preselection, (4) flexibility in designs/analyses, (5) financial/conflict interests, and (6) hot fields with many competing teams. Psi research exhibits ALL SIX risk factors: typical sample sizes n < 100 (very low power), effect sizes d = 0.1-0.3 (very small), many paradigms tested without correction, historical flexibility in protocols, strong beliefs on both sides, and active competition among labs. The Box 1 GWAS example (testing 100,000 genes for schizophrenia with only 10 true associations) directly parallels the multiple-testing problem in psi meta-analyses. This paper provides the mathematical foundation for skeptical arguments that small psi effects are almost certainly false positives [catalog ID: ioannidis_2005_false]
- Button et al. (2013) -- Analysis of 730 studies from 49 neuroscience meta-analyses finds median statistical power of 21% (18% excluding outliers); at this power level with pre-study odds of 1:4, the positive predictive value of a significant finding is only 50%; effect size inflation of 25-50% expected via the winner's curse; excess significance test confirms more significant results than expected (349 vs. 254, p < 0.0001); directly quantifies the power problem that Ioannidis (2005) theorized, with implications for all small-N psi research [catalog ID: button_2013_power_failure]
- Open Science Collaboration (2015) -- The reproducibility crisis in psychology is directly relevant: many psi effects are similar in size to mainstream psychology effects that have failed to replicate [catalog ID: open_science_2015_reproducibility]
- Lakens (2017) -- Equivalence testing methods show that many "significant" psi effects are practically equivalent to zero [catalog ID: lakens_2017_equivalence]
- Kennedy (2004) -- Proposes FDA-style pivotal study protocols for psi research, noting that 70-80% of studies in psi meta-analyses are non-significant and z scores fail to increase with sample size in RNG and ganzfeld research — defeating the assumptions underlying both power analysis and meta-analysis [catalog ID: kennedy_2004_proposal]
- Kennedy (2013) -- Compiles replication rates across major psi meta-analyses (ganzfeld, RNG, precognition, PK-dice), finding only 20-33% of well-conducted studies significant versus the 0.80 confirmatory standard; shows ganzfeld needs N=201 for adequate power yet median is 40 trials (power 0.22); RNG z-values are independent of sample size, contrary to statistical theory; recommends prospective registration, adequate power, and best-evidence synthesis over retrospective meta-analysis [catalog ID: kennedy_2013_beyond_meta_analyses]
- Kennedy (2014) -- "Bayesian and Classical Hypothesis Testing: Practical Differences for a Controversial Area of Research" argues that both methods are valid when properly applied but psychology/parapsychology lack confirmatory methodology; demonstrates that a uniform Bayesian prior biases against small effects (5100/10000 hits: classical p=.046 but BF=10.8 for null); recommends FDA-style protocols specifying priors, acceptance criteria, and operating characteristics prospectively; contends the absence of practical psi applications despite sufficient effort is indirect evidence against the effect [catalog ID: kennedy_2014_nature_psi]
- Kennedy (2016) -- Identifies eight methodological deficiencies unaddressed by the post-Bem methodological revolution: deficient study registration, dropout/incomplete data bias, absent software validation, no fraud prevention measures, inappropriate confirmatory statistics, Bayesian inferential errors, retrospective vs. prospective meta-analysis weaknesses, and statistical dependence in outcome variables; argues psychologists will inevitably choose methodology reform over accepting psi, making proactive adoption of FDA-level clinical trial standards the efficient path [catalog ID: kennedy_2016_psi_research_ready]
- Watt & Kennedy (2017) -- Proposes "registration-based prospective meta-analysis" where study inclusion decisions are made at pre-registration, before data collection; argues retrospective meta-analyses are essentially post-hoc exploratory analyses that have failed to resolve the ganzfeld, PK, and other parapsychological debates despite decades of effort; initiates an exemplar prospective meta-analysis for ganzfeld studies at the KPU Study Registry with power of 0.95 and a fixed total sample size to prevent bias from interim result tracking [catalog ID: watt_kennedy_2017_prospective_meta]
- Bierman (2003) -- "Does Consciousness Collapse the Wave Packet?" EEG experiment (N=30) testing the von Neumann-Wigner subjective reduction hypothesis using radioactive decay events; found significant pre-observer effects in early brain components (overall exact binomial p=0.0115) [catalog ID: bierman_2003_does]
- Schlitz, Wiseman, Watt & Radin (2006) -- Third collaborative study designed to explain the experimenter effect found in 1997 and 1999 (Schlitz significant, Wiseman null): the 2×2 cross-over design (N=100) produced no significant effect for anyone — neither the greeter nor sender role, nor their interaction, reached significance. Schlitz's replication condition (acting as both greeter and sender, as in 1997) yielded es=−0.03, p=.87. The authors conclude that "the inconsistent nature of our findings does not allow for a firm acceptance or rejection of either interpretation" — the effect may be genuine but disrupted by uncontrolled factors, or the earlier results may have been chance findings or artifacts [catalog ID: schlitz_2006_two_minds]
- Bösch, Steinkamp & Boller (2006) -- Independent meta-analysis of 380 RNG-PK studies in *Psychological Bulletin* demonstrates that a simple publication bias simulation can reproduce the observed small effect, extreme heterogeneity, and small-study effect; the most thorough skeptical treatment of the PK evidence base, prompting Radin et al.'s rebuttal in the same journal [catalog ID: bosch_2006_examining]
- Houran, Lange & Hooper (2018) -- Commentary proposing three criteria precognition evidence must satisfy: (a) replicable effects, (b) effect sizes exceeding measurement artifacts, (c) physics-consistent explanatory models; argues Mossbridge & Radin's review fails all three; includes direct assessment from a Fermi Lab theoretical astrophysicist [catalog ID: houran_2018_crossexamining]
- Watt & Kennedy (2015) -- Opinion article on practical lessons from operating the KPU study registry (2012-2014); recommends classifying analyses as exploratory or confirmatory, pre-specifying all decisions for confirmatory research, and making registrations irreversibly public before data collection; critiques OSF for allowing private/revocable registrations [catalog ID: watt_2015_lessons]
- Protzko & Schooler (2017) -- Taxonomy of four types of decline effects (false positive, inflated, under-specified, genuinely decreasing) with mechanisms including underpowered studies, publication bias, and selective reporting; Protzko argues decline effects are largely Type I errors amplified by confirmation bias, while Schooler entertains unconventional observer-related mechanisms; the conventional side of this analysis provides a comprehensive framework for explaining psi decline effects without invoking anomalous processes [catalog ID: protzko_2017_decline_effects]
- Ioannidis (2012) -- "Why Science Is Not Necessarily Self-Correcting" presents a taxonomy of six discovery–replication paradigms estimating that unchallenged fallacies (~53%) and unconfirmed genuine discoveries (~43%) dominate, with actual self-correction occurring in ≤1% of cases; only 1.07% of psychology papers are replications, and with 35% average power, 30–95% of significant findings may be false positives [catalog ID: ioannidis_2012_science_not_self_correcting]
- Pashler & Wagenmakers (2012) -- Editorial framing the replication crisis, citing Bem's (2011) ESP study and Simmons et al.'s (2011) false-positive psychology as catalysts for methodological reform; contextualizes the broader skeptical response to psi within the reform movement [catalog ID: pashler_2012_replicability_crisis]
- Wagenmakers et al. (2012) -- Proposes preregistration as the primary remedy for psychology's confusion of exploratory and confirmatory research; introduces the exploratory-confirmatory continuum framework; illustrates with a preregistered Bayesian replication of Bem's Experiment 1 (N=100, BF01 = 16.6 favoring null); argues that without preregistration, statistical tests are invalid because data have been 'used more than once' [catalog ID: wagenmakers_2012_agenda]
- Kennedy (2017) -- Drawing on firsthand experience exposing the Levy fraud and 20 years in FDA-regulated research, argues that post hoc fraud investigations are uniquely ineffective in parapsychology because data anomalies can be attributed to psi; advocates making fraud by one experimenter very difficult or impossible through duplicate records, dual-experimenter oversight, and documented audit trails — the pharmaceutical standard [catalog ID: kennedy_2017_experimenter_fraud]
- Kennedy (2024) -- "Addressing Researcher Fraud: Retrospective, Real-Time, and Preventive Strategies," *Frontiers in Research Metrics and Analytics*. Policy review arguing that researcher fraud is easy, enticing, and common in academic science. Analyzes three anti-fraud strategies: (1) retrospective investigations — limited by post-hoc bias, the low 'preponderance of evidence' legal standard, inability to identify perpetrators when data access is uncontrolled, and litigation risk (Gino v. Harvard: $25M defamation suit); (2) real-time sting operations — conclusive but rarely feasible; (3) preventive data management from FDA-regulated clinical trials — 8 practices largely absent from academic research: secure raw-data archiving, audit trails, restricted access, software validation, quality control, blinding with pre-registered analysis programs, research audits, and public raw data. Proposes an 'error-controlled data management' badge. Applied directly to parapsychology: Kennedy audited the Transparent Psi Project (Kekecs 2023) and found a significant software error even in that high-quality registered study; argues that most academic psi research lacks the data safeguards standard in pharmaceutical trials [catalog ID: kennedy_2024_research_fraud]
- Kennedy (2024) -- "Planning Falsifiable Confirmatory Research" in *Psychological Methods* (APA): argues that falsifiable research requires prespecified minimum effect sizes, power >= .95, and preregistered criteria for evidence both for and against hypotheses; shows that for d = 0.20, N >= 1,084 is needed for adequate falsifiable designs; notes Bem's (2011) precognition meta-analysis had median retrospective power of 7.7%; retrospective meta-analyses are essentially exploratory. Directly formalizes the argument that most psi research is unfalsifiable because it lacks specified effect sizes below which the hypothesis would be rejected [catalog ID: kennedy_2024_falsifiable_research]
- Hinterberger (2010) -- Three sequential EEG correlation studies using 750–800 km separation find a weak but replicable accumulated Alpha band effect (z=4.0, p=0.00003) that the author states survives even highly conservative multiple-testing correction; however, most individual significances across the full parameter space would not survive correction; no global ERP, SCP, or checkerboard replication found; discusses both artifact explanation and generalized entanglement interpretation [catalog ID: hinterberger_2010_searching]
- Simmons, Nelson & Simonsohn (2011) -- "False-Positive Psychology" demonstrates via simulation and experiment that common researcher degrees of freedom inflate false-positive rates to 60.7% when combined; produced significant evidence (p=.040) for an impossible hypothesis; the concept of "researcher degrees of freedom" provides the methodological framework for critiquing analytic flexibility across all psi paradigms [catalog ID: simmons_2011_false_positive]
- Gelman & Loken (2013) -- "Garden of Forking Paths" argues that the multiple comparisons problem arises even without deliberate fishing or p-hacking: data-contingent analysis choices (data exclusion, main effects vs. interactions, variable coding) create implicit multiplicity; uses Bem (2011) as a central example, showing that many alternative data patterns would have equally supported the precognition hypothesis via different but equally reasonable analysis choices [catalog ID: gelman_2013_forking_paths]
- John, Loewenstein & Prelec (2012) -- Survey of 5,964 academic psychologists using Bayesian Truth Serum (BTS) incentives found 94% admitted to at least one questionable research practice (QRP): 66.5% failed to report all dependent measures, 58% collected extra data after checking significance, 50% selectively reported studies that 'worked', and ~1 in 10 are estimated to have falsified data; items formed a Guttman scale (reproducibility=0.80), suggesting researchers share norms about QRP severity but differ on where to draw the line for themselves; provides empirical grounding for the claim that QRPs are the de facto scientific norm rather than rare exceptions [catalog ID: john_2012_questionable_practices]
- Ambach (2008) -- Failed replication of Wackermann et al.'s (2003, 2004) EEG correlation paradigm (17 pairs, shielded rooms, checkerboard reversals) that also discovered the original bootstrap sampling method systematically overestimates effects (false positive rate 20.1-26.2% at nominal alpha = 0.05); after correction, all six tests yielded p > .067; the uncorrected method applied to the same data would have produced apparently significant results (p = .0003) [catalog ID: ambach_2008_correlations]
- Bierman, Spottiswoode & Bijl (2016) -- Monte Carlo simulation of seven QRPs applied to 78 post-1985 ganzfeld experiments using a genetic algorithm: with realistic QRP prevalence rates from JLP (2012) surveys, QRPs account for ~60% of the reported effect; the widely cited meta-analytic p~10⁻¹⁶ reduces to p=0.003 after QRP correction; demonstrates that quantitative QRP simulation can assess meta-analytic inflation, applicable beyond parapsychology [catalog ID: bierman_2016_questionable_practices]
- Dean et al. (2022) -- Systematic review of 71 studies (N=20,993) on paranormal belief and cognition: only 7% included a-priori power analyses, 3% preregistered, 17% corrected for multiple comparisons despite averaging 43 p-values per study, 63% used undergraduate samples; documents systematic methodological weaknesses across 40 years of research [catalog ID: dean_2022_paranormal_beliefs_cognition]
- van Elk et al. (2015) -- Re-analysis of Shariff et al. (2015) religious priming meta-analysis (92 studies) using PET-PEESE and Bayesian bias correction: PET-PEESE finds no effect after correcting for publication bias (b0 = -0.002, p = 0.97), BBC finds strong evidence for real effect (~0.3); argues contradictory results prove meta-analysis alone cannot resolve disputed effects due to inability to disentangle true effects from publication bias and experimenter bias; concludes only preregistered large-scale replications can establish contested effects [catalog ID: van_elk_2015_registered_replications]

**Current status:** This meta-debate is the most consequential in the field because it determines whether psi research is capable of producing reliable knowledge.

The strongest pro-psi argument is Cardena's (2018) [catalog ID: cardena_2018_experimental] American Psychologist review, which was peer-reviewed by a mainstream journal and argues the evidence would be accepted in any other domain.

The strongest skeptical argument has two strands: (1) Reber & Alcock's (2019) [catalog ID: reber_2019_searching] contention that no statistical evidence can overcome the violation of known physics, and (2) Ioannidis's (2005) [catalog ID: ioannidis_2005_false] argument that small effects (d=0.1-0.3), small samples (n<100), multiple paradigms, flexible protocols, and strong beliefs create conditions where PPV < 50%.

The Schlitz–Wiseman series [catalog ID: wiseman_schlitz_1997_experimenter_staring] raises a deeper problem: experimenter bias may be built into psi research itself. In 1997 and 1999, the same experiment produced opposite results depending on who ran it — a psi-believing experimenter got significant skin conductance changes while a skeptical experimenter got nothing, using identical protocols. If the experimenter's beliefs can determine the outcome, it may be impossible to preserve objectivity via the exclusion of experimenter intention from the experiment. A 2006 follow-up [catalog ID: schlitz_2006_two_minds] designed to resolve the question found no effect for anyone — but the authors themselves concluded that "the inconsistent nature of our findings does not allow for a firm acceptance or rejection of either interpretation," leaving open whether the earlier results reflected genuine psi disrupted by uncontrolled factors or were artifacts.

The debate is unlikely to be resolved by any single experiment and may ultimately require either a physical mechanism for psi or a truly independent, large-scale, pre-registered replication effort.
